# Milton Friedman — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Economist · United States
Born: Brooklyn, New York, 31 July 1912 · Died: San Francisco, 16 November 2006

---

## Doctrine Summary

Friedman's intellectual contribution rests on three foundational arguments: that inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon — caused by excessive money supply growth, not by cost-push factors or structural conditions; that central banks and governments systematically make economic conditions worse through activist intervention, because they act on lagged and incomplete information; and that free markets are the most powerful mechanism for promoting both economic prosperity and individual freedom, and that the two are inseparable. He was the most influential economist of the second half of the 20th century in terms of direct policy impact — his monetarist framework replaced Keynesian demand management as the dominant central bank doctrine, and his advocacy of school vouchers, negative income tax, volunteer military, and drug legalisation shaped policy debates for decades. Unlike Hayek, who operated primarily as a political philosopher, Friedman was a technical economist who made policy arguments in the language of empirical economics. Their frameworks are complementary but distinct.

## T1 — Intellectual Landmark Decisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. A Monetary History of the United States (1963, with Anna Schwartz)**
Friedman and Schwartz's landmark work argued that the Federal Reserve caused the Great Depression by allowing the money supply to collapse by one-third between 1929 and 1933. This directly contradicted the Keynesian narrative (that the Depression was caused by insufficient aggregate demand requiring fiscal stimulus) and provided the empirical foundation for monetarism.
Source: Friedman and Schwartz, A Monetary History of the United States 1867–1960 (Princeton, 1963); Ben Bernanke's documented acknowledgment at Friedman's 90th birthday: "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry."

**2. The Natural Rate of Unemployment — AEA Presidential Address (1968)**
Friedman's presidential address to the American Economic Association argued that there is no long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment — only a temporary one. Attempts to hold unemployment below its natural rate produce accelerating inflation. This demolished the theoretical basis for Keynesian fine-tuning and anticipated the stagflation of the 1970s with precision.
Source: Friedman, "The Role of Monetary Policy," American Economic Review 58(1), 1968.

**3. Capitalism and Freedom (1962)**
Friedman's popular statement of his political economy — arguing that economic freedom is a necessary condition of political freedom, and that government programmes consistently produce unintended consequences that make them counterproductive. Introduced the negative income tax as an alternative to the welfare bureaucracy.
Source: Friedman, Capitalism and Freedom (University of Chicago Press, 1962).

**4. The Chile advisory role (1975)**
Friedman visited Chile in 1975 and met with Pinochet. His letter to Pinochet advising "shock treatment" — rapid economic liberalisation — was implemented by the "Chicago Boys." This is the most documented and most contested practical application of his framework.
Source: Friedman's letter to Pinochet, 21 April 1975 (published); documented in Juan Gabriel Valdés, Pinochet's Economists (1995); Friedman's own subsequent defence.

**5. Free to Choose — television series and book (1980)**
Friedman's popular television series and accompanying book with Rose Friedman brought his ideas to a mass audience, directly influencing the Reagan and Thatcher programmes. The most effective mass communication of free-market economics in the 20th century.
Source: Free to Choose (Harcourt, 1980); documented audience figures and policy influence.

**6. Advocacy for a volunteer military (1969)**
Friedman's testimony before the Gates Commission argued that a volunteer professional military would be more efficient and no less effective than conscription — an argument that directly influenced the Nixon administration's decision to end the draft.
Source: Gates Commission testimony 1969; documented in David Henderson, "Milton Friedman's Volunteer Military."

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Positions
*Confidence: [documented]/[inferred]*

**Monetary policy:**
- Documented position: central banks should follow a fixed rule for money supply growth rather than discretionary policy — because discretionary policy consistently destabilises rather than stabilises [documented] (A Program for Monetary Stability, 1960)
- Documented evolution: later in life, Friedman acknowledged that targeting money supply growth was less practical than he had thought — monetary velocity was less stable than his models assumed [documented]
- Documented opposition to fixed exchange rates and advocacy for floating exchange rates [documented]

**Social policy:**
- Negative income tax: documented advocacy for replacing the welfare bureaucracy with a direct cash transfer to all citizens below a threshold — a position that anticipated universal basic income debates [documented] (Capitalism and Freedom Chapter 12)
- School vouchers: documented advocacy from 1955 onwards — government should finance education but not necessarily provide it [documented]
- Drug legalisation: documented advocacy — the war on drugs fails by Friedman's own cost-benefit framework [documented]

**Intellectual positions:**
- Documented positivist methodology: "The ultimate goal of a positive science is the development of a theory or hypothesis that yields valid and meaningful predictions about phenomena not yet observed." Essays in Positive Economics (1953) [documented]
- Documented position: a theory should be judged by the accuracy of its predictions, not the realism of its assumptions [documented] — a methodological position that drew sustained criticism

## T3 — Stated Positions
*Indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: [documented] unless noted*

### Monetary Policy and Inflation
- "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." A Monetary History and multiple subsequent sources. [documented]
- "The Federal Reserve definitely caused the Great Depression." Multiple sources — the core claim of A Monetary History. [documented]
- Documented position: central banks should follow rules rather than discretion — k-percent rule for money supply growth prevents the destabilising effects of activist monetary policy. [documented]
- "There's no such thing as a free lunch." Multiple sources — his most quoted aphorism, applied consistently to government programmes that claim to provide benefits without costs. [documented]

### Markets and Freedom
- "The society that puts equality before freedom will end up with neither. The society that puts freedom before equality will end up with a great measure of both." Free to Choose (1980). [documented]
- Documented position: economic freedom and political freedom are inseparable — no society has sustained political freedom without also relying primarily on market mechanisms. [documented] (Capitalism and Freedom)
- "Concentrated power is not rendered harmless by the good intentions of those who create it." Capitalism and Freedom. [documented]
- Documented position: most government interventions produce the opposite of their intended effects because they ignore price signals and create perverse incentives. [documented]

### Welfare and Social Policy
- Documented position: the negative income tax is superior to categorical welfare because it preserves work incentives while eliminating the welfare bureaucracy. [documented] (Capitalism and Freedom Chapter 12)
- Documented position: the licensing of professions (doctors, lawyers, teachers) primarily protects incumbents rather than consumers and should be substantially reduced. [documented]
- On school choice: "Vouchers would improve education not primarily because of competition but because they would give parents power to choose — and choice is itself a good." [inferred from documented positions]

### Macroeconomic Policy
- Documented position: fiscal policy (government spending and taxation) is an ineffective tool for macroeconomic stabilisation — lags are too long and political processes too slow. [documented]
- "Only a crisis — actual or perceived — produces real change. When that crisis occurs, the actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying around." Capitalism and Freedom preface. [documented]

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. Chile — liberty and authoritarianism**
Friedman argued that economic freedom and political freedom are inseparable. He then advised a military dictatorship on economic policy and publicly defended his advice as producing economic benefits for Chileans. This contradicts his foundational argument in Capitalism and Freedom. He maintained that economic liberalisation would eventually produce political liberalisation — a prediction that proved partially correct but on a much longer timescale than he implied, and after severe human rights violations.
Source: Friedman's Chile letter 1975; Capitalism and Freedom Chapter 1; documented critique in Naomi Klein, The Shock Doctrine (2007); Friedman's documented responses.

**2. Money supply targeting — practical revision**
Friedman's core policy recommendation was to target a fixed rate of money supply growth. When this was actually implemented (by Volcker at the Fed in 1979–82), it proved extremely difficult to execute in practice because monetary velocity was unstable. Friedman later acknowledged that money supply targeting was less implementable than he had argued.
Source: Documented in Edward Nelson, "Milton Friedman and U.S. Monetary History," Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis Review (2007).

**3. Assumptions and predictions — the methodology debate**
Friedman's methodological position — that theories should be judged by predictive accuracy, not realistic assumptions — was used to defend models with unrealistic assumptions about human behaviour. When these models produced poor predictions (as in the 2008 financial crisis), the methodology provided no internal corrective mechanism.
Source: Friedman, "The Methodology of Positive Economics" (1953); documented critique in Paul Samuelson and others.

**4. Corporate social responsibility**
Friedman's 1970 essay "The Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase Its Profits" argued that corporate executives who pursue social goals with shareholders' money are spending money that is not theirs. This position has been extensively criticised as contributing to short-termism and environmental neglect. Friedman maintained it was logically correct.
Source: Friedman, "The Social Responsibility of Business," New York Times Magazine, 13 September 1970.

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The Chile advisory role — moral failure**
Whatever the economic outcomes in Chile, Friedman's advice to and defence of the Pinochet regime — which tortured and killed thousands of political opponents — represents a documented moral failure. His argument that economic liberalisation would produce political liberalisation proved empirically incomplete: political freedom returned to Chile decades later, not as a direct consequence of his reforms.
Source: Documented human rights record of Pinochet regime; Friedman's letters and interviews.

**2. The 2008 financial crisis**
The deregulatory framework that Friedman's work supported contributed to the conditions that produced the 2008 financial crisis. The Federal Reserve's post-2001 monetary loosening followed Friedman's prescription to prevent deflation — and produced the asset price inflation that preceded the crisis. Friedman died in 2006 before the crisis but his framework did not predict or prevent it.
Source: Multiple post-crisis assessments; Ben Bernanke's documented acknowledgment of the Friedman/Schwartz framework's influence.

**3. Money supply targeting in practice**
The Volcker disinflation of 1979–82, which applied Friedman's monetarist framework, successfully reduced inflation but at the cost of a severe recession with unemployment reaching 10.8%. The human cost was significant and documented. Friedman supported the policy but acknowledged the difficulty of execution.
Source: Federal Reserve records 1979–82; documented unemployment and GDP data.

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Friedman when the issue involves:**
- Inflation, monetary policy, and the role of central banks
- The limits of government intervention in markets
- School choice, vouchers, and competition in public services
- Welfare design — cash transfers vs in-kind benefits
- Corporate governance and the purpose of the firm
- Any question about whether a government programme is likely to produce its intended effects or their opposite
- Free trade and the case against protectionism

**Do not select Friedman when the issue primarily involves:**
- Post-conflict institution building — not his framework
- Rights and justice as primary concerns — he subordinates these to market outcomes
- Development economics for poor countries — his framework assumes functioning market institutions
- Geopolitics and security — not his domain

**Critical pairing note — Keynes, Hayek, Friedman:**
These three form the essential triangle of 20th-century economic thought. Keynes: government must manage aggregate demand. Hayek: government cannot possess the knowledge required to intervene effectively. Friedman: government intervention is not merely ignorant but actively destabilising. In any major economic policy session, having at least two of the three produces a genuine intellectual debate. Having all three is ideal.

**Critical note — Chile:**
The Chile advisory role must not be omitted. A Friedman profile without it is incomplete and misleading. His argument that economic freedom produces political freedom is the central theoretical claim of Capitalism and Freedom — and Chile is the documented case that most challenges it.
