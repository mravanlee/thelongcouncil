# Franklin D. Roosevelt — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Practitioner · President, United States 1933–45
Born: Hyde Park, New York, 30 January 1882 · Died: Warm Springs, Georgia, 12 April 1945

---

## Doctrine Summary

Roosevelt's governing philosophy rests on four documented convictions: that the state has an affirmative obligation to protect citizens from economic catastrophe — not merely to set the framework and step aside; that political leadership in a crisis requires decisive action even when the correct action is uncertain; that coalition-building across incompatible interests is the essential skill of democratic governance; and that the international order requires active American stewardship, not withdrawal. He was the most consequential democratic leader of the 20th century — he governed through the Great Depression and the Second World War, created the institutional architecture of the American welfare state, and designed the post-war international order (Bretton Woods, the United Nations). His record is among the most extensively documented of any political leader: twelve years of presidential papers, fireside chats, press conference transcripts, cabinet records and diplomatic cables.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The First Hundred Days — emergency banking legislation and the New Deal framework (March–June 1933)**
In his first hundred days, Roosevelt signed fifteen major pieces of legislation including the Emergency Banking Act, the Glass-Steagall Act, the Agricultural Adjustment Act, and the National Industrial Recovery Act. The speed and breadth were deliberate — Roosevelt argued that visible government action was as important as the specific content of each measure. Restoring confidence was itself a policy objective.
Source: Roosevelt, First Inaugural Address, 4 March 1933; Presidential papers 1933; Arthur Schlesinger Jr., The Age of Roosevelt vol.2 (1959) pp.1–21.

**2. Taking the US off the gold standard (April 1933)**
Roosevelt's executive order prohibiting gold hoarding and suspending gold convertibility — breaking with the gold standard — was taken against Treasury advice and orthodox economic opinion. It was one of the most consequential monetary decisions of the 20th century, enabling the monetary expansion that ended deflation.
Source: Executive Order 6102, 5 April 1933; Barry Eichengreen, Golden Fetters (1992) pp.322–339; Roosevelt's personal diary entries on the decision.

**3. The Social Security Act (August 1935)**
Roosevelt's decision to create a contributory social insurance system rather than a means-tested welfare programme was a specific design choice with documented rationale. He wanted beneficiaries to feel they had earned their benefits — making it politically durable. "Nobody is ever going to take away their social security."
Source: Social Security Act, 14 August 1935; Roosevelt's documented explanation of the contributory design; Schlesinger vol.2 pp.308–315.

**4. Lend-Lease (March 1941)**
Roosevelt's decision to supply Britain with war material without immediate payment — before the US entered the war — required circumventing the Neutrality Acts. His political management of the decision — including the "garden hose" analogy in press conferences — is documented as a masterclass in preparing a democracy for a policy it was not yet ready to accept.
Source: Lend-Lease Act, 11 March 1941; Roosevelt press conference transcripts; Doris Kearns Goodwin, No Ordinary Time (1994) pp.183–210.

**5. Bretton Woods — designing the post-war international order (July 1944)**
Roosevelt approved and backed the Bretton Woods conference that created the IMF and World Bank. His framework for the post-war order — international institutions rather than bilateral great-power deals — represented a decisive break with Wilsonian failure and isolationism.
Source: Bretton Woods Conference records July 1944; Benn Steil, The Battle of Bretton Woods (2013); Roosevelt's documented instructions to the US delegation.

**6. The Four Freedoms (January 1941)**
Roosevelt's State of the Union articulating four fundamental freedoms — freedom of speech, freedom of worship, freedom from want, freedom from fear — as the basis of American foreign policy aims. A documented statement of the ideological framework for US international engagement.
Source: Roosevelt, State of the Union Address, 6 January 1941; Presidential papers.

**7. Japanese American internment (February 1942)**
Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066 authorising the internment of Japanese Americans — a documented decision that violated the civil liberties of 120,000 people, the majority of them US citizens. He later acknowledged the decision as a war measure; he never formally apologised.
Source: Executive Order 9066, 19 February 1942; documented in Greg Robinson, By Order of the President (2001).

**8. The decision not to bomb the railway lines to Auschwitz (1944)**
Roosevelt's War Department declined repeated requests to bomb the railway lines leading to Auschwitz concentration camp. The decision — documented in War Department records — has been extensively debated. Officials argued the bombers were needed elsewhere; critics have documented that missions flew within range of the lines repeatedly.
Source: War Department records 1944; David Wyman, The Abandonment of the Jews (1984); documented in Roosevelt presidential papers.

**Economic management:**
- Decision to experiment — the New Deal was explicitly not a single coherent plan but a series of experiments, some of which were abandoned when they failed [documented] (Roosevelt, multiple press conferences)
- The 1937 recession — Roosevelt's decision to reduce deficit spending in 1937, producing a sharp recession, then reversing course. A documented case of learning from error [documented]
- Documented tolerance of internal contradictions in the New Deal — programmes that contradicted each other economically were retained if they were politically necessary [inferred from documented pattern]

**Political management:**
- The court-packing plan (1937) — Roosevelt's attempt to expand the Supreme Court after it struck down New Deal legislation; it failed politically and damaged his domestic position [documented]
- Coalition management: the New Deal coalition held together Southern segregationists, Northern labour unions, and urban immigrants simultaneously [documented] — a documented tolerance of moral compromise for political coalition purposes
- The fireside chats — deliberate use of radio to communicate directly with citizens, bypassing hostile press [documented]

**Foreign policy:**
- Decision to recognise the Soviet Union (1933) — a specific, documented diplomatic choice [documented]
- Atlantic Charter with Churchill (1941) — documented framework for post-war international order before US entry into the war [documented]

## T3 — Stated Positions
*Indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: [documented] unless noted*

### Economic Crisis Management
- "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself." First Inaugural Address, 4 March 1933. [documented]
- "I am fighting Communism, Nazism, Fascism, Huey Longism. I want to save our system, the capitalistic system." Documented private statement — he framed the New Deal explicitly as saving capitalism from its own excesses. [documented] (Schlesinger vol.2)
- Documented position: government action in a depression is not socialism — it is the insurance that capitalism requires to survive. [inferred from multiple documented sources]
- On the 1937 recession: "I made a mistake by moving too fast towards a balanced budget." Documented acknowledgment of error. [documented]

### The Role of the State
- "Government is not the master of the people. It is the servant of the people." Multiple sources. [documented]
- Documented position: freedom from want is as important as freedom of speech — political freedom means nothing to a man who is starving. [documented] (Four Freedoms speech)
- On Social Security design: "Those taxes were never a problem of economics. They are politics all the way through. We put those payroll contributions there so as to give the contributors a legal, moral, and political right to collect their pensions... With those taxes in there, no damn politician can ever scrap my social security program." [documented]

### International Order
- Documented position: the United States cannot remain isolated from events in Europe and Asia — American security depends on the character of the international order. [documented] (multiple fireside chats and speeches 1937–41)
- On the United Nations: "We are not fighting for conquest. We are fighting for a world in which this nation, and all that this nation represents, will be safe for our children." [documented]
- Documented preference for multilateral institutions over bilateral great-power arrangements — documented in contrast to Churchill's preference for spheres of influence. [documented] (Yalta Conference records)

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. Civil liberties — Japanese internment**
Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech articulated freedom as the foundational American value. Executive Order 9066 interning Japanese Americans violated the civil liberties of 120,000 people six weeks later. He never reconciled these positions publicly.
Source: Four Freedoms speech January 1941; Executive Order 9066 February 1942.

**2. Fiscal policy — the 1937 reversal**
Roosevelt campaigned in 1936 on balancing the budget and implemented spending cuts in 1937. The economy immediately contracted sharply. He reversed course, acknowledging the error. This is a documented case of learning through failure — but also of applying orthodox fiscal thinking at exactly the wrong moment.
Source: Budget documents 1937; Roosevelt's documented acknowledgment of error.

**3. The New Deal and racial equality**
Roosevelt built the New Deal coalition partly by accommodating Southern segregationist Democrats — meaning New Deal programmes systematically excluded or underpaid Black Americans. Agricultural workers and domestic servants (disproportionately Black) were excluded from Social Security. He consistently prioritised coalition over principle on race.
Source: Documented exclusions in New Deal legislation; Ira Katznelson, Fear Itself (2013).

**4. Isolationism to internationalism**
Roosevelt ran for re-election in 1940 pledging to keep America out of war while simultaneously moving towards intervention. He documented this as political management — the public needed time to accept what was strategically necessary. Critics have called it deception.
Source: 1940 campaign speeches vs Lend-Lease and Atlantic Charter; documented in Robert Dallek, Franklin D. Roosevelt and American Foreign Policy (1979).

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. Japanese internment**
Executive Order 9066 is the most clearly documented moral failure of Roosevelt's presidency. The internment of 120,000 Japanese Americans — based on race, not individual evidence of disloyalty — was subsequently ruled unconstitutional and resulted in a formal government apology and reparations in 1988.
Source: Executive Order 9066; Civil Liberties Act 1988; documented in Robinson (2001).

**2. The 1937 recession**
Roosevelt's premature move toward fiscal austerity in 1937 produced a sharp economic contraction that erased much of the New Deal's employment gains. It took a year of renewed deficit spending to recover. He acknowledged the error — but the human cost of the contraction was real and documented.
Source: Economic statistics 1937–38; Roosevelt's documented acknowledgment.

**3. Failure to act on the Holocaust**
The documented decision not to prioritise rescue of European Jews — including declining to bomb Auschwitz railway lines, refusing to increase immigration quotas, and the turning back of the MS St. Louis — represents a documented failure of moral imagination and political will.
Source: Wyman (1984); State Department records; documented in Richard Breitman and Allan Lichtman, FDR and the Jews (2013).

**4. The court-packing plan**
Roosevelt's 1937 attempt to expand the Supreme Court from nine to fifteen justices — after it struck down New Deal legislation — was a documented political failure. It was defeated in Congress, damaged his domestic coalition, and is widely considered an overreach that weakened his second term.
Source: Congressional records 1937; Schlesinger vol.3.

## Member Selection Notes

**Select FDR when the issue involves:**
- Economic crisis management — recession, depression, demand collapse
- The state's role in protecting citizens from economic catastrophe
- Designing social insurance and welfare architecture
- International institution building — when and how to create multilateral frameworks
- Leadership in a democracy during crisis — how to move faster than public opinion
- Coalition building across incompatible interests
- Any question about whether government intervention saves or distorts capitalism

**Do not select FDR when the issue primarily involves:**
- Monetary policy detail — his gold standard decision was intuitive, not theoretically grounded
- Post-colonial governance — not his framework
- Technology and AI — no documented positions
- Rights and justice as primary frameworks — he consistently subordinated these to political coalition

**Critical note:**
FDR's moral failures — internment, Holocaust response, racial exclusions in the New Deal — must not be omitted. A Roosevelt who is only the New Deal and the Four Freedoms is a myth. The tension between his genuine commitment to economic freedom and his tolerance of racial injustice is essential context for any session where he sits.
