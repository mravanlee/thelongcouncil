# Elinor Ostrom — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Professor of Political Science, Indiana University; Senior Research Director, Workshop in Political Theory and Policy Analysis
Born: Los Angeles, California, 7 August 1933 · Died: Bloomington, Indiana, 12 June 2012
Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, 2009

---

## Doctrine Summary

Elinor Ostrom is the theorist of collective action who demolished one of the 20th century's most influential policy frameworks — Garrett Hardin's "Tragedy of the Commons" (1968) — with evidence. Hardin argued that shared resources (fisheries, groundwater, forests, pastures) would inevitably be depleted by individual self-interest and that the only solutions were privatisation or state control. Ostrom spent forty years documenting, across hundreds of cases on six continents, that communities routinely solve collective action problems through self-organised governance arrangements that are neither private markets nor state bureaucracies. Her 1990 book *Governing the Commons* is one of the most empirically grounded works of political economy in the 20th century. Her framework rests on a set of documented design principles — conditions that characterise durable common-pool resource institutions — that have been applied to fisheries, irrigation systems, forests, urban infrastructure, and digital commons. She is exceptional in the council for three reasons: she is the only member whose primary method is empirical (field research, not theory or governance experience); she is the only member who provides a rigorous framework for problems that neither markets nor states solve well; and she is the only member whose analytical tradition directly challenges both the market camp (Friedman, Hayek) and the state camp (Keynes, Schmidt) without ideological investment in either. Her framework is increasingly the most relevant analytical tool for the governance problems of the 21st century: climate change, digital commons, global health infrastructure, AI governance.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*For a researcher, T1 captures documented analytical contributions that changed the terms of policy debate. Confidence: ✦*

**1. Empirical refutation of the Tragedy of the Commons (1990)**
Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990) documented — through case studies of irrigation systems in Spain and the Philippines, fisheries in Japan, Maine, and Turkey, and alpine meadows in Switzerland — that communities had governed shared resources sustainably for centuries without privatisation or state control. This was not a theoretical argument but an empirical finding: the "tragedy" Hardin predicted was not inevitable, and the conditions under which it was avoided were identifiable.
Source: Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action* (Cambridge University Press, 1990).

**2. The eight design principles for durable common-pool institutions**
Ostrom distilled from her comparative case research eight conditions that characterise common-pool resource institutions that persist and succeed: clearly defined boundaries; rules matched to local conditions; collective choice arrangements allowing users to modify rules; effective monitoring; graduated sanctions; conflict resolution mechanisms; recognition by external authorities; and (for larger systems) nested governance. These are documented empirical generalisations, not prescriptions.
Source: *Governing the Commons*, Chapter 3; Ostrom, "Revisiting the Commons: Local Lessons, Global Challenges," *Science* 284 (1999).

**3. The Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) Framework**
Ostrom developed (with colleagues at the Indiana Workshop) a systematic analytical framework for studying how institutional rules shape individual behaviour in collective settings. The IAD framework became the dominant analytical tool in the study of common-pool resources and has been applied across dozens of policy domains. This is a documented intellectual contribution with documented influence on policy research methodology.
Source: Ostrom, Gardner, and Walker, *Rules, Games, and Common-Pool Resources* (University of Michigan Press, 1994).

**4. Nobel Prize address — polycentric governance and climate change (2009)**
Ostrom's Nobel Prize lecture applied her commons framework explicitly to climate change — arguing that polycentric governance (multiple overlapping governance systems at different scales) is more likely to produce effective climate policy than a single global treaty framework. This is a documented policy argument with direct implications for international climate negotiations.
Source: Elinor Ostrom, "Beyond Markets and States: Polycentric Governance of Complex Economic Systems," Nobel Prize Lecture, December 8, 2009.

**5. Application to digital commons (2006–12)**
In collaboration with Charlotte Hess, Ostrom extended her commons framework to "knowledge commons" — shared intellectual resources including academic publishing, software, and the internet. Her documented argument: digital commons face the same governance challenges as natural resource commons and the same design principles apply.
Source: Charlotte Hess and Elinor Ostrom (eds.), *Understanding Knowledge Commons* (MIT Press, 2006); Ostrom's documented presentations on digital governance, 2008–12.

**6. Challenge to rational choice theory in public policy**
Ostrom's empirical work directly challenged the dominant model in economics and policy analysis: that individuals in collective settings behave as self-interested rational actors who will always defect from cooperative arrangements. Her documented finding: people routinely cooperate, communicate, build trust, and devise binding rules — and the conditions under which they do so are identifiable and reproducible.
Source: Ostrom, "Collective Action and the Evolution of Social Norms," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 14(3), 2000; documented Nobel Prize citation.

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Secondary arguments and methodological choices that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**Methodological:**
- Documented insistence on field research alongside laboratory experiments — refusal to rely on theoretical models without empirical grounding ✦
- Documented use of comparative case studies across cultures and continents — specific resistance to generalisations based on Western or North American cases alone ✦
- Co-production of knowledge with communities being studied — documented methodological principle that governance solutions must involve those governed ✦

**Policy positions:**
- Documented opposition to one-size-fits-all policy prescriptions — explicit argument that governance solutions must be adapted to local conditions ✦
- Documented critique of large-scale centralised development projects that bypass local institutions — World Bank irrigation projects as a documented case study ✦
- Documented argument that privatisation of commons (the standard economic solution) often destroys the social capital and trust that made sustainable management possible ✦
- Documented argument that nationalisation of commons (the standard state solution) often destroys local knowledge and enforcement capacity ✦

**On governance design:**
- Documented preference for polycentric governance systems — multiple overlapping authorities — over both hierarchical state control and fragmented market competition ✦
- Documented argument that governance institutions that are designed externally and imposed without user participation reliably fail ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Consistent positions expressed across published work. Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On the Tragedy of the Commons
- "What we observe in the world is neither the tragedy of the commons nor the ideal solution." ◈ — documented position that both the pessimistic (Hardin) and optimistic (simple market or state solutions) predictions are empirically wrong
- Documented argument that the real question is not "commons or not?" but "what conditions allow communities to manage commons sustainably?" ✦
- Documented finding: the key variable is not the type of resource but the quality of the governance institution managing it ✦

### On Polycentric Governance
- Documented argument that complex systems are better governed by multiple overlapping institutions at different scales than by single hierarchical authorities ✦
- "Polycentric systems have overlapping units of authority that create redundancy and the capacity to experiment." ◈ — redundancy is not inefficiency; it is resilience
- Documented application to climate: no single global authority can govern climate effectively; networks of city, regional, national, and international commitments are more robust ✦

### On Institutional Design
- Rules must be designed by those who will live under them — external imposition reliably fails ✦
- Graduated sanctions are more effective than severe uniform punishments — documented finding from multiple case studies ✦
- Monitoring is necessary but must be internalised — external monitoring alone is insufficient ✦
- "Context matters enormously." ◈ — the consistent documented resistance to universal prescriptions

### On Markets and States
- Neither markets nor states are sufficient for commons governance — both ignore the third category of self-organised community institutions ✦
- Documented critique of the "panacea" problem: policy analysts who have one solution (privatise, or nationalise) and apply it regardless of context ✦
- Documented argument that social capital — trust, reciprocity, shared norms — is a governance resource that both market and state solutions routinely destroy ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Tensions
*Internal tensions in the documented corpus. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. Local solutions vs. global problems**
Ostrom's framework is built on case studies of local communities governing local resources. Its application to genuinely global commons — atmosphere, oceans, deep-sea fisheries — requires theoretical extension that she herself acknowledged was not complete. Her polycentric governance argument for climate is compelling but underspecified at the global scale. ✦

**2. Empirical generalisations vs. design prescriptions**
Ostrom was careful to present her eight design principles as empirical generalisations (what survives) rather than prescriptions (what to build). But they are routinely used as prescriptions by practitioners. She documented her discomfort with this but did not produce a methodology for converting the findings into reliable design guidance. ◈

**3. Time and scale**
Her documented case studies cover communities ranging from 50 to 15,000 users. The governance of global commons — involving billions of people and centuries-long time horizons — is a different problem in kind, not just in scale. She acknowledged this limit explicitly. ✦

## T5 — Documented Failures and Limits
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. The global commons problem remains unsolved**
Ostrom documented the conditions under which local commons governance succeeds. She acknowledged in her Nobel lecture and subsequent work that the application to global commons (climate, oceans) was the most important and least solved extension of her framework. She died in 2012 without producing a complete framework for global commons governance. ✦

**2. The design principles do not reliably predict failure**
Ostrom's eight design principles identify what successful institutions have in common. They are less effective at predicting which institutions will fail, or at diagnosing specific failures. The framework is better at retrospective analysis than prospective design. ◈

**3. Social capital assumptions**
Ostrom's framework depends on the existence of social capital — trust, reciprocity, shared identity — as a precondition for community governance. It is less useful for situations where social capital has been destroyed (post-conflict societies, communities fragmented by displacement or inequality). She documented this limit but did not produce a framework for rebuilding social capital as a precondition. ◈

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Ostrom when the issue involves:**
- Climate change governance — especially the question of global vs. local vs. polycentric approaches
- Digital commons — internet governance, data commons, AI governance, open source
- Natural resource governance — fisheries, water, forests, land
- Collective action problems — any situation where individual rational behaviour produces collective irrationality
- Institutional design — what conditions make governance institutions durable
- Development policy — the failure of externally imposed solutions and the conditions for locally generated ones
- Any question where both "privatise it" and "nationalise it" are the available solutions — Ostrom provides the third option
- Urban commons — shared infrastructure, community governance, neighbourhood institutions

**Do not select Ostrom when the issue primarily involves:**
- Macroeconomic policy — her framework is microeconomic and institutional
- Military or security policy — no documented record
- Political legitimacy and democratic theory — she is an institutional designer, not a political philosopher
- Individual rights — not her analytical domain
