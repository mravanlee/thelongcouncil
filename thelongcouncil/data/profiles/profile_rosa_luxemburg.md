# Rosa Luxemburg — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Revolutionary theorist, political economist, anti-war activist · Co-founder, Spartacus League (1916) · Co-founder, Communist Party of Germany (KPD, 1918) · Professor, Social Democratic Party school, Berlin (1907–14)
Born: Zamość, Russian Poland, 5 March 1871 · Died: Berlin, Germany, 15 January 1919 (murdered by Freikorps officers during the Spartacist uprising)

---

## Doctrine Summary

Rosa Luxemburg is the most rigorous Marxist theorist of the relationship between capitalism, imperialism, democracy, and revolutionary action — and the only figure in the council whose framework applies both a systematic critique of capitalist accumulation and a systematic critique of the authoritarian tendencies within socialist revolution itself. Born into a Jewish family in Russian-occupied Poland, she completed a doctorate in political economy at the University of Zurich in 1898 (one of the first women to do so in German-speaking Europe), and became the leading theoretical voice of the left wing of German Social Democracy before World War I. Her framework rests on four documented convictions: that capitalism requires continuous geographical expansion into non-capitalist economies to survive (the imperialism argument, developed in *The Accumulation of Capital*, 1913); that the trade-union and electoral politics of reformist socialism cannot overcome capitalism because capitalism's contradictions are structural, not political (the critique of Bernstein); that revolutionary transformation without democratic participation produces dictatorship, not socialism (the critique of Lenin and the Bolsheviks); and that war between capitalist states is not an accident or policy failure but the predictable consequence of imperialist competition. She spent a third of her adult life in prison — for opposing German militarism, for opposing World War I, for "inciting class hatred." She was murdered at forty-seven by right-wing Freikorps officers during the failed Spartacist uprising in Berlin, fifteen months after escaping prison at the war's end. Her body was thrown into the Landwehr Canal. She is unique in the council for four reasons: she is the only member who produced systematic economic theory of capitalist crisis and imperialism from within the Marxist tradition; the only member who simultaneously opposed both bourgeois liberalism and Bolshevik authoritarianism from a documented democratic position; the only member whose framework centres the contradiction between economic emancipation and political freedom as inseparable requirements; and the only member killed by the state for the positions she held. Her framework is directly relevant to 21st-century questions about the structural tendencies of global capitalism, the relationship between economic inequality and democratic erosion, the political economy of war and militarism, and the conditions under which revolutionary movements preserve or destroy democratic freedoms.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*For a revolutionary theorist, T1 captures documented interventions that changed the framing of Marxist politics. Confidence: ✦*

**1. The critique of Bernstein and the Revisionism debate (1899)**
In *Social Reform or Revolution?* (1899), the twenty-eight-year-old Luxemburg demolished Eduard Bernstein's revisionist argument that capitalism had stabilised and that socialism could be achieved incrementally through trade unions and parliamentary reform. Her documented argument: capitalism's contradictions are structural and reproduce themselves through growth; reforms improve conditions but do not address the mechanism of exploitation; the choice between reform and revolution is not a tactical question but a question about whether capitalism can be transformed at all. This intervention established her as the leading theoretical voice of the SPD's left wing before she was thirty.
Source: Rosa Luxemburg, *Sozialreform oder Revolution?* (Leipzig, 1899); English: *Reform or Revolution*, in Peter Hudis and Kevin B. Anderson (eds.), *The Rosa Luxemburg Reader* (Monthly Review Press, 2004).

**2. *The Accumulation of Capital* and the theory of imperialism (1913)**
Luxemburg's *Die Akkumulation des Kapitals* (1913) is the most systematic documented argument for the structural inevitability of imperialism. Her analytical move: Marx's reproduction schemes in Volume II of *Capital* assume a closed capitalist economy, but capitalism has never existed in pure form — it requires continuous expansion into non-capitalist sectors (peasant economies, colonial societies) to realise surplus value. Imperialism is therefore not a policy choice but the economic form of capitalism's late stage. When the non-capitalist periphery is exhausted, capitalism must either collapse or reproduce itself through the destruction of value (war). This is the first rigorous documented economic theory of imperialism from within the Marxist tradition and remains a central reference in contemporary political economy.
Source: Rosa Luxemburg, *Die Akkumulation des Kapitals: Ein Beitrag zur ökonomischen Erklärung des Imperialismus* (Vorwärts, Berlin, 1913); English: *The Accumulation of Capital* (Monthly Review Press, 1951/2003).

**3. The Junius Pamphlet and the opposition to World War I (1915)**
Written in prison under the pseudonym "Junius," *The Crisis of German Social Democracy* (1915) is the most rigorous documented Marxist analysis of the SPD's August 1914 vote in favour of war credits — which Luxemburg considered the moral collapse of European social democracy. Her documented argument: the war was not a defensive response to Russian aggression but the predictable outcome of imperialist competition; the SPD's support for the war was not a tactical error but a betrayal of the working class's interest in peace. Her famous formulation: "Bourgeois society stands at the crossroads, either transition to socialism or regression into barbarism." This is the original documented "socialism or barbarism" argument.
Source: Rosa Luxemburg, *Die Krise der Sozialdemokratie* (the Junius Pamphlet), Zurich, 1916; English: *The Crisis in the German Social Democracy*, in *The Rosa Luxemburg Reader* (2004).

**4. The critique of the Russian Revolution (1918)**
Written in prison in September–October 1918, Luxemburg's *The Russian Revolution* is the earliest documented Marxist critique of Bolshevik authoritarianism from a position of solidarity rather than hostility. Her documented argument: she supported the October Revolution against its liberal and conservative critics, but insisted that the Bolsheviks' suppression of democratic freedoms — dissolution of the Constituent Assembly, restriction of press freedom, one-party rule — was not a temporary tactical necessity but a structural mistake that would transform socialism into bureaucratic dictatorship. Her formulation: "Freedom only for the supporters of the government, only for the members of one party — however numerous they may be — is no freedom at all. Freedom is always the freedom of the dissenter." This anticipates by a decade the critiques later made by Trotsky in exile.
Source: Rosa Luxemburg, *Zur russischen Revolution*, written September–October 1918, published posthumously by Paul Levi in 1922; English: *The Russian Revolution*, in *The Rosa Luxemburg Reader* (2004).

**5. The Spartacus League and the founding of the KPD (1918–19)**
After her release from prison in November 1918, Luxemburg co-founded the Spartacus League (Spartakusbund) with Karl Liebknecht, and in December 1918 the Communist Party of Germany (KPD). Her documented role: drafting the KPD's founding programme, which combined revolutionary commitment with an explicit rejection of the Bolshevik single-party model. Against Liebknecht and the majority of the founding congress, she argued against the KPD boycott of the January 1919 elections to the National Assembly — her documented position was that revolutionary socialists must engage with mass democratic processes even while pursuing revolutionary transformation.
Source: Rosa Luxemburg, "What Does the Spartacus League Want?" (December 1918), in *The Rosa Luxemburg Reader* (2004); Paul Frölich, *Rosa Luxemburg: Her Life and Work* (Left Book Club, 1940).

**6. The Spartacist uprising and her murder (January 1919)**
The Spartacist uprising in Berlin, 5–15 January 1919, was a failed workers' insurrection that Luxemburg had argued against as premature — her documented position was that the German working class was not yet ready for a successful revolution. She nonetheless stood with the insurgents once it began, on documented grounds of revolutionary solidarity. Arrested with Liebknecht on 15 January by Freikorps officers under the command of the Social Democratic government, she was beaten with rifle butts, shot, and her body thrown into the Landwehr Canal, where it was not recovered until May. Her killing represents the documented limit of her theoretical framework: the democratic revolutionary position she held could be physically destroyed by the alliance of the social-democratic state with paramilitary right-wing forces — an alliance that her framework had predicted but that she did not survive.
Source: Klaus Gietinger, *The Murder of Rosa Luxemburg* (Verso, 2019); German federal court records released in the 1920s investigation.

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Secondary arguments and methodological choices that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**On method:**
- Documented insistence on the unity of theory and practice — she refused the division between academic Marxism and political activism, arguing both were necessary and that their separation produced bad theory and bad politics ✦
- Consistent documented practice of engaging her Marxist opponents (Bernstein, Lenin, Kautsky) at the level of their strongest arguments — her critiques are forensic rather than polemical ✦
- Documented preference for the essay and pamphlet form over the systematic treatise — she produced one major economic work (*The Accumulation of Capital*) and otherwise wrote occasional pieces, correspondence, and journalism ◈

**On revolutionary organisation:**
- Documented opposition to Lenin's "democratic centralism" as developed in *What Is to Be Done?* (1902) — Luxemburg's 1904 critique in "Organizational Questions of Russian Social Democracy" argued that the revolutionary party must remain accountable to the working class's own democratic processes, not substitute for them ✦
- Consistent documented argument for the mass strike as the characteristic form of modern revolutionary action — neither a tactical instrument deployed by party leadership nor a spontaneous eruption, but a dialectical process in which mass action and political consciousness develop together ✦
- Documented critique of trade-union bureaucracy as having developed institutional interests distinct from the working class it claimed to represent — a documented parallel to her later critique of party bureaucracy ✦

**On war and militarism:**
- Documented argument that war is not an external threat to capitalist societies but their internal logic made explicit — militarism is "the provider of capitalism in excess production" ✦
- Consistent documented opposition to "defensive" wars as a category — she argued that under imperialism no European power was engaged in genuinely defensive warfare ✦
- Documented critique of patriotic mobilisation as the technique by which capitalist states secure working-class consent to wars that are against working-class interests ✦

**On democracy and dictatorship:**
- Documented argument that the historic mission of the working class is to create a more democratic society, not a less democratic one — dictatorship in the service of socialism is a contradiction ✦
- Consistent documented position that freedoms of press, assembly, and speech are not bourgeois luxuries but preconditions for the mass political consciousness that revolutionary transformation requires ✦
- Documented insistence that a socialist society must include democratic mechanisms for correcting its own errors — because the transition to socialism cannot be planned in advance but must be discovered through collective action ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Consistent positions expressed across the oeuvre. Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On Capitalism and Imperialism
- Documented argument that capitalism cannot reproduce itself on a purely capitalist base — it requires continuous expansion into non-capitalist economies to realise surplus value and sustain accumulation ✦
- "Capital cannot accumulate without the aid of non-capitalist organisations, nor can it tolerate their continued existence side by side with itself." *The Accumulation of Capital* (1913). ◈
- Consistent documented position that imperialism's destructive phase is not a policy failure that could be reformed but the endpoint of capitalism's logic — when the non-capitalist periphery is exhausted, accumulation can only proceed through the destruction and reconstruction of value (war) ✦

### On Reform and Revolution
- Documented argument that reforms and revolutionary transformation are not alternatives but sequentially related — reforms develop the working class's capacities, but only revolutionary transformation addresses the underlying mechanism of exploitation ✦
- "Legislative reform and revolution are not different methods of historic development that can be picked out at the pleasure of the buffet of history... but different moments in the development of class society which condition and complement each other." *Reform or Revolution* (1899). ◈
- Consistent documented critique of reformist socialism as having mistaken the instruments (trade unions, parliamentary representation) for the goal (the transformation of the relations of production) ✦

### On Revolution and Democracy
- Documented argument that socialist revolution cannot be conducted against democracy without destroying itself — the freedoms of speech, press, and assembly are necessary conditions for the working class's political self-education ✦
- "Freedom is always, and exclusively, freedom for the one who thinks differently." *The Russian Revolution* (1918). ◈
- Consistent documented position that the suppression of dissent within the revolutionary camp produces not socialism but a new ruling bureaucracy — the October Revolution's democratic failures were not temporary but structural ✦

### On the Mass Strike and Political Agency
- Documented argument that the mass strike is the characteristic form of modern revolutionary action — neither a tactical instrument wielded by party leadership nor a spontaneous eruption but a dialectical process in which mass action and political consciousness develop together ✦
- Consistent documented position that revolutionary consciousness cannot be brought to the working class from outside (contra Lenin) but develops through mass action itself — the party's role is to articulate and generalise what the class discovers in struggle ✦
- "The mass strike is not artificially 'made,' not 'decided' at random, not 'propagated,' but it is a historical phenomenon." *The Mass Strike, the Political Party and the Trade Unions* (1906). ◈

### On War and Internationalism
- Documented argument that working-class internationalism is not a moral aspiration but a material interest — workers of different nationalities share interests that are fundamentally opposed to those of their own ruling classes ✦
- Consistent documented opposition to the "national question" as it was typically framed — she argued that national independence within the capitalist system does not produce genuine self-determination for workers, and that socialist internationalism must not be compromised by nationalist movements ✦
- Documented formulation: "Socialism or barbarism" — the historical choice was not between two forms of progress but between socialist transformation and the collapse into destructive war ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Tensions
*Documented position changes and internal tensions. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. Mass spontaneity vs. revolutionary leadership**
Luxemburg argued that revolutionary consciousness develops through mass action, not through vanguard leadership. She also argued that revolutionary parties must provide theoretical articulation and strategic coherence to mass movements. The relationship between spontaneity and leadership is documented as a dialectic but is not specified mechanically — when mass action and party strategy conflict (as they did in Germany in January 1919, when she considered the Spartacist uprising premature but participated), her framework does not provide clear guidance. ✦

**2. The national question**
Luxemburg's documented opposition to Polish and other nationalist movements within the Russian and Austro-Hungarian empires was one of her most contested positions. Against Lenin, who supported the right of nations to self-determination, she argued that this would split the international working class along national lines. Her position has been defended and criticised from within the Marxist tradition ever since — the criticism being that she underestimated the specifically colonial and national dimensions of oppression. ✦

**3. The critique of Lenin — applied selectively?**
Luxemburg's 1918 critique of the Bolsheviks was sharp on the suppression of democratic freedoms. But she also defended the October Revolution against its bourgeois critics and joined the Communist International's early formation. The tension between her democratic critique of Bolshevism and her solidarity with the Russian revolution is documented but underdeveloped — she was killed before the Stalinist degeneration she had anticipated could be addressed in her own work. ✦

**4. The Accumulation of Capital — the underconsumption debate**
Luxemburg's argument in *The Accumulation of Capital* — that capitalism requires non-capitalist sectors to realise surplus value — was contested within Marxist economic theory from its publication. Critics including Nikolai Bukharin argued that Luxemburg had misunderstood Marx's reproduction schemes. The debate continues, and her specific argument about the mechanism of imperialism remains technically disputed even among those who share her political conclusions. ✦

**5. Socialism or barbarism — determinism or contingency?**
Her formulation of "socialism or barbarism" as the historical choice has been read alternately as a deterministic prediction and as a contingent appeal for action. Her own texts support both readings — sometimes she writes as though capitalism's collapse is inevitable and the only question is what replaces it; sometimes she writes as though collapse into barbarism is a live possibility that depends on political action. The tension reflects a genuine uncertainty in her framework. ◈

## T5 — Documented Failures and Limits
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. The Spartacist uprising and strategic misjudgement**
Luxemburg argued against the Spartacist uprising in January 1919 as premature, then participated in it once it began. The uprising failed, she was murdered, and the KPD was deprived of its founding leadership. Her documented preference for mass democratic process was overridden by the logic of revolutionary solidarity in the moment of decision. The tension between her strategic judgment and her political loyalty was not resolved — it was ended by her killing. ✦

**2. The underestimation of nationalism's durability**
Luxemburg's documented opposition to nationalist movements within multinational empires assumed that working-class internationalism would overcome national consciousness. The twentieth century demonstrated the opposite — nationalism proved more durable than class solidarity in most of the world. Her framework addresses this limit partially (she recognised the SPD's nationalist capture in 1914) but does not provide a systematic theory of why internationalism failed. ✦

**3. The gap between economic theory and political prescription**
*The Accumulation of Capital* is a major theoretical achievement but its translation into political strategy is incomplete. If capitalism's collapse is structurally determined, the question of revolutionary agency becomes harder to formulate. Luxemburg addresses this in her occasional writings but does not produce a systematic account of how economic crisis translates into political transformation. ◈

**4. Limited engagement with the colonial periphery**
Luxemburg's framework centres European capitalism and its relationship with non-European peripheries, but her analysis of those peripheries remains external. She does not engage with the political and cultural agency of colonised peoples as subjects of their own history — this is a gap that Fanon's framework fills. Her treatment of non-European societies as objects of capitalist expansion is theoretically sophisticated but politically limited. ✦

**5. The organisational question unresolved**
Luxemburg opposed both the SPD's bureaucratic reformism and the Bolsheviks' authoritarian centralism. She did not produce a positive organisational theory that specifies how a revolutionary party can remain democratically accountable to the working class while providing strategic coherence. The question of revolutionary organisation in the twentieth century was settled by Lenin's model (in the affirmative) or by social-democratic reform (in the negative); Luxemburg's third way remains theoretically underdeveloped. ◈

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Luxemburg when the issue involves:**
- The structural tendencies of capitalist accumulation — crisis, imperialism, uneven development
- The relationship between economic inequality and democratic erosion
- War, militarism, and the political economy of conflict between major powers
- The critique of socialist or revolutionary movements that produce new forms of authoritarianism
- Reform versus structural transformation as strategic questions
- Trade unions, labour movements, and their institutional tendencies
- The conditions under which revolutionary transformation preserves democratic freedoms
- International financial capitalism and its relationship with geopolitical competition
- The political economy of the Global South's integration into world capitalism
- The limits of parliamentary politics as an instrument of transformation

**Do not select Luxemburg when the issue primarily involves:**
- Gradualist or consensus-based policy reform within stable institutions
- Technocratic economic management or macroeconomic fine-tuning
- Questions that assume the permanence of the capitalist framework
- Military strategy in the narrow operational sense
- Religious or cultural questions outside the political-economic frame
- Individual rights questions outside the structural context of class and exploitation
- Post-national or purely transnational governance questions — her framework is state-centred even in its internationalism
