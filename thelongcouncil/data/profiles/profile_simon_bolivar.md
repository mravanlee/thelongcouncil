# Simón Bolívar — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Practitioner · Military and Political Leader · President of Gran Colombia 1819–30; President of Bolivia 1825–26; President of Peru 1824–26
Born: Caracas, Venezuela, 24 July 1783 · Died: Santa Marta, Colombia, 17 December 1830

---

## Doctrine Summary

Simón Bolívar is the dominant figure of Latin American independence and the most instructive case study in the region's political history: a liberator who won military campaigns across a continent, designed republics, wrote constitutions, and then watched the political order he built fragment within his lifetime. His thinking evolved through three documented phases that are all still relevant. First: idealist republicanism — the belief, drawn from Enlightenment sources and Rousseau, that liberated peoples would govern themselves through rational institutions. Second: hard realism — the documented conclusion, reached through the failure of the First and Second Venezuelan Republics, that Spanish colonial society had not produced the educated, politically experienced citizenry that republican government required. Third: authoritarian pragmatism — his 1826 Bolivia Constitution, which proposed a lifetime presidency and a hereditary Senate, reflecting his documented conclusion that strong executive authority was the only stable foundation for new states. Bolívar never resolved the tension between his republican ideals and his authoritarian conclusions — and neither has Latin America in the two centuries since. His *Carta de Jamaica* (1815) and the *Angostura Address* (1819) are the foundational documents of Latin American political thought. He is the indispensable voice on any question about what follows liberation, why post-independence states fragment, and why regional integration fails.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*Directly documented decisions traceable to specific moments and sources. Confidence: ✦*

**1. The Admirable Campaign and recapture of Caracas (1813)**
After the collapse of the First Venezuelan Republic, Bolívar launched the Admirable Campaign from New Granada — a 75-day military operation that recaptured Caracas with a small force through speed, decisiveness, and political symbolism. His documented proclamation of "War to the Death" (*Guerra a Muerte*) — declaring that Spaniards who did not actively support independence would be killed — was a documented strategic decision to eliminate the middle ground and force a binary choice on the population. Morally controversial; militarily effective.
Source: Proclamation of War to the Death, Trujillo, June 15, 1813; Daniel Florencio O'Leary, *Bolívar and the War of Independence* (University of Texas Press, 1970).

**2. The Carta de Jamaica (1815)**
Written in exile in Kingston after the collapse of the Second Republic, the *Carta de Jamaica* is Bolívar's most important single political document. It analyses why Spanish colonial rule had failed to produce self-governing capacity, why Spanish America would fragment into multiple republics rather than unify, and what institutional design would be required for stability. He was largely right about the fragmentation and largely wrong about the institutional fix. Documented as the founding text of Latin American political analysis.
Source: Simon Bolívar, *Carta de Jamaica* (Kingston, September 6, 1815); Harold Bierck (trans.), *Selected Writings of Bolívar* (Colonial Press, 1951).

**3. Angostura Address and the Congress of Angostura (1819)**
Bolívar's address to the Congress of Angostura — convened to design the constitution for the new republic — is the most complete documented statement of his political philosophy. He argued against copying the US and French constitutions directly, insisted on a powerful executive as a stabilising force, proposed a hereditary Senate as a counterweight to popular passion, and introduced a "Moral Power" — a fourth branch of government responsible for civic education and public virtue. The Congress rejected the most radical proposals but adopted the framework for Gran Colombia.
Source: Simon Bolívar, *Angostura Address*, February 15, 1819; John Lynch, *Simón Bolívar: A Life* (Yale University Press, 2006) pp.134–142.

**4. Battle of Boyacá (1819) — liberation of New Granada**
The decisive military victory that established the viability of Gran Colombia. Bolívar's documented strategic decision — to cross the Andes in winter, taking a route considered impassable, to attack the Spanish from an unexpected direction — is studied as a case of calculated strategic surprise. The human cost (hundreds died in the crossing) was accepted as a documented military calculation.
Source: O'Leary memoirs; Lynch (2006) pp.125–130; Venezuelan National Archive.

**5. The Bolivia Constitution (1826)**
Bolívar's most controversial documented political act: a constitution for the newly created Bolivia that proposed a lifetime president (himself), a tricameral legislature with a hereditary Senate, and a highly restricted franchise. His documented rationale: the new states lacked the social and educational prerequisites for fully competitive democracy and needed a stabilising authority. The constitution was rejected in most of Gran Colombia but implemented briefly in Bolivia and Peru.
Source: Simon Bolívar, *Bolivia Constitution* (1826); David Bushnell, *The Liberator* (2003) pp.145–160.

**6. Dissolution of Gran Colombia and the Ocaña Convention (1828)**
When Gran Colombia — the unified state encompassing modern Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, and Panama — began to fragment under separatist pressure, Bolívar convened the Ocaña Convention (1828) to redesign the constitutional framework. When the Convention failed to produce agreement, he dissolved it and assumed dictatorial powers by decree. This is a documented decision that contradicted his republican principles — he acknowledged the contradiction explicitly in private correspondence.
Source: Decree of Organic Act, August 27, 1828; Lynch (2006) pp.210–225; Bolívar correspondence with General Santander.

**7. Resignation and death (1830)**
Facing military revolt, political isolation, and the definitive fragmentation of Gran Colombia, Bolívar resigned the presidency on March 1, 1830 — the most documented exercise of voluntary power relinquishment in Latin American history. He died nine months later of tuberculosis, politically defeated. His documented final letters are among the most candid post-mortems of political failure by a major leader.
Source: Bolívar's resignation address, March 1, 1830; final correspondence, November–December 1830; García Márquez, *The General in His Labyrinth* (fiction but based on documented sources).

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Secondary decisions that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**Military strategy:**
- Documented use of llanero cavalry (mixed-race plainsmen) after initial reliance on Creole elites — a documented shift toward building a genuinely popular military force ✦
- Decision to seek British mercenaries and financial support — documented recognition that external resources were necessary to sustain a long campaign ✦
- Documented willingness to execute subordinate commanders who disobeyed orders, including documented cases during the campaign ✦

**Political design:**
- Documented insistence on abolishing slavery in territories under his control — documented ahead of most of his contemporaries and against significant Creole landowner resistance ✦
- Documented rejection of constitutional monarchy as a model — he had access to the Haitian precedent and the European monarchies; he chose republicanism despite his doubts about its viability ✦
- Documented attempt to create a Pan-American Congress (Panama Congress, 1826) — a documented precursor to the Organisation of American States, largely a failure in his lifetime ◈

**Personal conduct:**
- Documented use of emergency powers on multiple occasions justified by military necessity — each time with documented stated intention to return to constitutional government ✦
- Documented refusal of personal enrichment — contemporary accounts and his own documented estate confirm he died without significant personal wealth ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Consistent positions expressed across the Carta de Jamaica, Angostura Address, and correspondence. Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On Republican Government and Its Prerequisites
- "A people that loves freedom will in the end be free." *Carta de Jamaica* (1815). ✦ — but immediately qualified by his documented argument that freedom requires civic capacity that Spanish colonialism had systematically destroyed
- Documented argument that the colonial experience — 300 years of political exclusion, restricted education, and dependence on Spanish administration — had left Spanish American societies without the institutional memory or civic habits that self-government requires ✦
- "We are not Europeans; we are not Indians; we are but a mixed species of aborigines and Spaniards." *Angostura Address* (1819). ✦ — documented argument for designing institutions suited to specific social realities, not imported models

### On Executive Power and Stability
- Documented argument that a strong executive is not the enemy of liberty but its precondition — weak executives produce caudillismo and civil war ✦
- Documented position that the US Constitution was not exportable: it had been produced by a specific Protestant, Anglo-Saxon, commercially experienced society and would not function in a different social context ✦
- "Good laws are nothing without good officials to enforce them." ◈ — consistent documented pattern of prioritising capable administration over constitutional elegance

### On Regional Unity
- Documented belief that Latin American fragmentation would leave all the new republics vulnerable to external domination — the argument for continental solidarity ✦
- Documented prediction that without unity, the new republics would fall under British economic domination or US political domination within a generation ✦ — prescient
- Panama Congress (1826): documented effort to create a permanent inter-American body; documented failure due to national rivalries he had predicted ✦

### On Slavery and Racial Equality
- Documented abolitionist position — consistently advocated for emancipation in territories under his control, citing both moral grounds and the military necessity of recruiting Afro-Latin soldiers ✦
- Documented tension between abolitionist principle and reliance on Creole landowner political support — a contradiction he managed but never resolved ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Documented position changes. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. Republican idealism to authoritarian pragmatism**
Bolívar's documented political evolution is the central case study of this tension in Latin American history. His 1815 *Carta de Jamaica* expresses genuine republican idealism; his 1826 Bolivia Constitution proposes a lifetime president and hereditary senate. He documented this shift explicitly: "I have come to the conclusion that America is ungovernable." (Letter to General Flores, November 9, 1830). ✦

**2. Liberator vs. dictator**
Bolívar assumed dictatorial powers by decree in 1828 while simultaneously writing that dictatorship was incompatible with republican government. He documented the contradiction in correspondence: he believed both positions simultaneously and found no resolution. ✦

**3. Abolitionism vs. political dependence on slaveholders**
Bolívar documented his abolitionist convictions consistently. He also documented his political dependence on Creole landowners — many of whom were slaveholders — for the economic and military resources of independence. The tension was never resolved in his lifetime; emancipation across South America followed his death over decades. ✦

**4. Continental unity vs. national sovereignty**
Bolívar argued simultaneously for Latin American unity and for the principle of national sovereignty that made unity structurally impossible. He never produced a documented resolution — the tension between integration and sovereignty that he identified in 1815 remains the central problem of Latin American politics. ✦

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: ✦*

**1. Fragmentation of Gran Colombia (1830)**
The unified state that was Bolívar's primary political project disintegrated into Venezuela, Colombia, and Ecuador within his lifetime. He documented this as his greatest failure. The causes were structural — geographic, economic, and political — but his own use of dictatorial powers accelerated the separatist forces he was trying to contain.
Source: Lynch (2006) pp.240–260; Bolívar correspondence 1828–30.

**2. The Bolivia Constitution — rejected and reversed**
The constitutional model Bolívar designed for Bolivia and attempted to extend across Spanish America was rejected by the societies he had liberated. His lifetime presidency proposal was seen — accurately — as inconsistent with republican principles. It was abolished in Bolivia within two years.
Source: Bushnell (2003) pp.155–165; documented congressional records, Lima and Bogotá.

**3. The Panama Congress (1826) — failure of regional integration**
Bolívar's 1826 Pan-American Congress, convened in Panama City, was attended by only four delegations and produced no binding agreements. The United States sent observers but did not join. The British sent a commercial agent. Bolívar's documented vision of a permanent inter-American body failed in his lifetime.
Source: Congress of Panama proceedings (1826); Arthur Whitaker, *The Western Hemisphere Idea* (Cornell, 1954).

**4. Dictatorship and the loss of legitimacy (1828–30)**
Bolívar's assumption of dictatorial powers in 1828 destroyed his republican legitimacy and accelerated the political collapse he was trying to prevent. His documented rationale — emergency necessity — was the same rationale used by every subsequent Latin American military dictator. He was aware of this and found no way out.
Source: Lynch (2006) pp.215–230; Bolívar's documented correspondence with Santander.

**5. Failure to institutionalise**
Bolívar's legacy was personal, not institutional. He built armies and won battles but did not build durable civilian institutions — courts, universities, tax administrations, free press — that would outlast his personal authority. This is a documented structural failure he acknowledged in his final correspondence.
Source: Bolívar's documented letters to General Flores and General O'Leary, 1830.

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Bolívar when the issue involves:**
- Post-independence or post-liberation political fragmentation — what follows liberation
- The prerequisites for self-governance — whether constitutional design can substitute for civic capacity
- Regional integration and the structural obstacles to it
- Strong executive authority as a stabilising force — the tension between republican principles and effective governance
- Latin America specifically — political culture, sovereignty, and external dependency
- The relationship between military liberation and civilian institution-building
- Any question about whether democratic models designed in one context can be transplanted to another

**Do not select Bolívar when the issue primarily involves:**
- Economic policy — his documented record on economics is thin
- Social policy or welfare — not his analytical domain
- Authoritarian development models — he is an opponent of authoritarianism who became authoritarian; he is a cautionary tale, not an advocate
- Post-industrial or technology governance — structural extrapolation required, flag as ◇
