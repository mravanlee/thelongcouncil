# John Rawls — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Political philosopher · United States
Born: Baltimore, Maryland, 21 February 1921 · Died: Lexington, Massachusetts, 24 November 2002

---

## Doctrine Summary

Rawls's contribution to political philosophy rests on one foundational thought experiment and three principles derived from it. The thought experiment: imagine designing the basic structure of society from behind a "veil of ignorance" — not knowing what position you would occupy in it (rich or poor, talented or not, majority or minority). The principles that rational individuals would choose from this original position: first, equal basic liberties for all; second, fair equality of opportunity; and third, the difference principle — social and economic inequalities are only justified if they benefit the least advantaged members of society. This is the most rigorous philosophical framework for asking the question: what does a just society look like? He also developed a theory of political liberalism — that in a pluralist society, political principles must be justifiable to all citizens regardless of their comprehensive moral or religious views. His work is fully documented across two major books and dozens of essays spanning forty years.

## T1 — Intellectual Landmark Decisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. A Theory of Justice (1971)**
Rawls's foundational work — one of the most influential works of political philosophy of the 20th century. The original position and veil of ignorance as a device for deriving principles of justice. The two principles of justice: (1) equal basic liberties; (2) fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle. A direct challenge to utilitarianism — justice cannot be sacrificed for aggregate welfare.
Source: Rawls, A Theory of Justice (Harvard University Press, 1971; revised edition 1999).

**2. The difference principle — formulation**
Rawls's most contested and most original contribution: inequalities in the distribution of social goods are only justified if they improve the position of the least advantaged members of society. This does not require equality of outcomes — it requires that inequality works for the worst-off, not merely that it is tolerated.
Source: A Theory of Justice §12–17; extensive scholarly discussion.

**3. Political Liberalism (1993)**
Rawls's major revision of A Theory of Justice — responding to the criticism that his theory of justice presupposed a comprehensive liberal moral framework that not all citizens share. Political liberalism argued that principles of justice must be freestanding — justifiable by an "overlapping consensus" of citizens with different comprehensive moral views.
Source: Rawls, Political Liberalism (Columbia University Press, 1993; expanded edition 1996).

**4. The Law of Peoples (1999)**
Rawls's extension of his theory to international relations — arguing for a "law of peoples" governing relations between liberal and non-liberal societies. Controversial among his followers: he argued that the difference principle does not apply globally, and that non-liberal but "decent" societies deserve tolerance from liberal ones.
Source: Rawls, The Law of Peoples (Harvard University Press, 1999); Thomas Pogge's critique.

**5. "Justice as Fairness: Political not Metaphysical" (1985)**
Rawls's documented self-revision — reframing his theory of justice as a political rather than a metaphysical doctrine. This essay marks the transition from Theory of Justice to Political Liberalism and represents a significant revision of his earlier work.
Source: Rawls, "Justice as Fairness: Political not Metaphysical," Philosophy & Public Affairs 14(3), 1985.

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Positions
*Confidence: [documented]/[inferred]*

**On economic institutions:**
- Documented position: A Theory of Justice is compatible with both a property-owning democracy and liberal socialism — it is not a defence of welfare-state capitalism as currently practiced [documented] (Theory of Justice; clarified in Justice as Fairness: A Restatement, 2001)
- Documented position: welfare-state capitalism is actually less just than a property-owning democracy, because welfare transfers income to the poor without distributing the ownership of productive assets [documented] (Justice as Fairness: A Restatement)
- Documented position: the difference principle requires asking not just whether the poor receive transfers but whether the basic structure of property ownership serves the least advantaged [documented]

**On pluralism and public reason:**
- Documented position: in a pluralist democracy, citizens have a duty to justify their political positions in terms that other citizens can accept — religious or comprehensive moral arguments are not adequate justifications for coercive law [documented] (Political Liberalism)
- Documented position: the overlapping consensus — the area where different comprehensive doctrines agree on political principles — is the legitimate basis of public political argument [documented]

**On international justice:**
- Documented position that the difference principle does not apply globally — a contested revision that his followers including Thomas Pogge explicitly rejected [documented] (Law of Peoples)
- Documented position: "decent" non-liberal societies deserve international tolerance if they respect basic human rights and maintain a rule of law [documented]

## T3 — Stated Positions
*Indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: [documented] unless noted*

### Justice and Inequality
- "Each person possesses an inviolability founded on justice that even the welfare of society as a whole cannot override." A Theory of Justice §1. [documented]
- "All social values — liberty and opportunity, income and wealth, and the social bases of self-respect — are to be distributed equally unless an unequal distribution of any or all of these values is to everyone's advantage." A Theory of Justice §11. [documented]
- The difference principle: "Social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged members of society." A Theory of Justice §13. [documented]
- Documented position: justice is the first virtue of social institutions — an unjust law or institution has no claim on our compliance, however efficient. [documented]

### Institutions and the Basic Structure
- Documented position: the primary subject of justice is the basic structure of society — the way major institutions distribute fundamental rights and duties. [documented] (Theory of Justice §2)
- Documented distinction: principles of justice apply to institutions, not directly to individual acts — we cannot derive a theory of personal morality from Rawls's political philosophy. [documented]
- Documented position: free and equal persons choosing principles of justice would choose principles that do not permit gambling with anyone's life prospects. [documented]

### Political Liberalism and Pluralism
- Documented position: political authority is legitimate only if its exercise can be justified to all citizens regardless of their comprehensive moral views. [documented] (Political Liberalism)
- "The idea of public reason applies to citizens when they engage in political advocacy in the public forum, and also to how they vote in elections." Political Liberalism (expanded edition). [documented]
- Documented position: religious arguments may motivate political action but must be accompanied by public reason justifications to be legitimate in a pluralist democracy. [documented]

### The Original Position
- Documented position: the original position is a device of representation, not a description of how decisions are actually made — it models what principles would be chosen by rational persons who did not know their social position. [documented]
- "The veil of ignorance prevents us from knowing our place in society, our class position or social status; nor does it know our fortune in the distribution of natural assets and abilities, our intelligence and strength, and the like." A Theory of Justice §24. [documented]

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. A Theory of Justice to Political Liberalism — the grounding question**
A Theory of Justice presented its principles as derived from rational choice under idealised conditions — implying a universal application. Political Liberalism revised this: the principles are freestanding, justified by overlapping consensus, and applicable only within liberal democratic societies. This is a significant contraction of the theory's scope. Rawls acknowledged the revision but argued it was a clarification rather than a contradiction.
Source: Political Liberalism Introduction; "Justice as Fairness: Political not Metaphysical" (1985).

**2. The difference principle — property-owning democracy vs welfare state**
A Theory of Justice was widely read as a philosophical justification for the welfare state. Rawls later clarified that he considered welfare-state capitalism less just than a property-owning democracy, because welfare capitalism concentrates ownership while redistributing income. This revision is documented but underappreciated.
Source: Justice as Fairness: A Restatement (2001) §41–43.

**3. The Law of Peoples — global justice**
Rawls's cosmopolitan followers (Pogge, Beitz) argued that his own theory of justice implied a global difference principle — that global inequalities are only justified if they benefit the globally worst-off. Rawls denied this in The Law of Peoples. The tension between his domestic theory and his international theory is a documented and unresolved contradiction.
Source: Thomas Pogge, Realizing Rawls (1989); Rawls, Law of Peoples; documented exchange.

**4. Civil disobedience**
Rawls argued that civil disobedience is justified when addressing substantial and clear injustice in a "nearly just" society. He did not adequately address cases of deep structural injustice — where the society is not "nearly just." This limitation is documented in feminist and critical race theory critiques.
Source: Theory of Justice §55–59; documented critique in Iris Marion Young, Justice and the Politics of Difference (1990).

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The ideal theory problem**
Rawls's theory is explicitly ideal theory — it describes principles for a perfectly just society in fully compliant conditions. Critics have documented that this makes it poorly equipped to guide policy in real societies with unjust histories, non-compliant actors, and path-dependent institutions. Rawls acknowledged the gap between ideal and non-ideal theory but never fully closed it.
Source: Amartya Sen, The Idea of Justice (2009) — the most systematic critique; documented in Rawls's incomplete response.

**2. The difference principle — measurement problem**
The difference principle requires identifying the least advantaged group and determining whether inequalities benefit them. Rawls never provided an adequate account of how to measure this in practice. The principle is philosophically rigorous but operationally indeterminate.
Source: Documented in Norman Daniels, ed., Reading Rawls (1975); multiple economic critiques.

**3. The Law of Peoples — tolerance of non-liberal regimes**
Rawls's argument that non-liberal but "decent" societies deserve international tolerance was criticised by his own followers as too permissive toward authoritarian governance. His criteria for "decency" were vague enough to accommodate regimes that his own domestic theory of justice would condemn.
Source: Thomas Pogge critique; Susan Moller Okin, "Is Multiculturalism Bad for Women?" — documented criticism of tolerance of non-liberal practices.

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Rawls when the issue involves:**
- The just distribution of social and economic goods — who benefits and who bears costs?
- Institutional design — what principles should govern the basic structure of a society or policy?
- Inequality — when is inequality justified and when is it not?
- Rights vs aggregate welfare — when do individual rights trump collective benefit?
- Political legitimacy in a pluralist society — what can be publicly justified to all citizens?
- Any question that requires asking: what would a fair procedure for this decision look like?

**Do not select Rawls when the issue primarily involves:**
- Crisis management requiring urgent action (his framework is deliberative and ideal, not crisis-oriented)
- Geopolitics — his international theory is his weakest area
- Economic policy mechanisms — he provides principles, not instruments
- Military strategy — not his domain

**Critical note — Rawls and Hayek:**
Rawls and Hayek represent the foundational tension in modern political philosophy on justice and markets. Rawls argues that market outcomes require justification by their effect on the least advantaged. Hayek argues that market outcomes cannot be judged just or unjust because they are not designed by any agent. Any session involving inequality, redistribution, or the just design of economic institutions benefits enormously from both.

**Critical note — ideal theory:**
Rawls's framework is explicitly designed for ideal conditions. When applying it to real policy questions, the [extrapolated] signal is frequently needed. His principles provide a normative benchmark; they do not provide a decision procedure for imperfect real-world choices. This must be acknowledged in any reasoning card where his framework is applied.
