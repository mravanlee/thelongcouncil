# John Locke — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Philosopher and political theorist · England
Born: Wrington, Somerset, 29 August 1632 · Died: Oates, Essex, 28 October 1704

---

## Doctrine Summary

Locke's political philosophy rests on three foundational claims: that legitimate government requires the consent of the governed; that individuals possess natural rights to life, liberty and property that no government can legitimately override; and that when a government systematically violates those rights, the people retain the right to dissolve it. He is the intellectual architect of liberal constitutionalism — the framework that underlies most democratic governance today. His work is simultaneously foundational and contested: the same texts have been read as a defence of property rights against redistribution, a justification for colonial expropriation, and a framework for revolutionary democracy. The confidence signal framework requires citing which reading, from which scholarly tradition.

**Note on T1/T2 for Framers:** Locke held no elected office. His "decisions" are intellectual — what to argue, what to revise, what to publish and when, and what to suppress. T1 captures his major intellectual interventions; T2 captures the secondary positions and private counsel that reveal his reasoning patterns.

## T1 — Intellectual Landmark Decisions
*Major documented intellectual interventions with traceable sources. Confidence: ✦*

**1. Two Treatises of Government — publication decision (1689)**
Locke wrote the Two Treatises in the late 1670s and early 1680s as a justification for resistance to Charles II. He chose to publish anonymously in 1689, after the Glorious Revolution, reframing them as a retrospective justification rather than a revolutionary manifesto. The timing and framing were deliberate strategic decisions documented in his correspondence.
Source: Locke, Two Treatises of Government (1689); Peter Laslett's critical edition (Cambridge, 1960) establishes the earlier composition date; Locke correspondence vol.3, Clarendon Press.

**2. Exclusion of Catholics and atheists from toleration**
The Letter Concerning Toleration (1689) explicitly argues for religious toleration but explicitly excludes Catholics (on political grounds — loyalty to a foreign prince) and atheists (on contractual grounds — oaths cannot bind those who deny divine sanction). This is not an oversight; it is a documented deliberate boundary.
Source: Locke, A Letter Concerning Toleration (1689); scholarly analysis in John Marshall, John Locke: Resistance, Religion and Responsibility (Cambridge, 1994).

**3. The labour theory of property — first formulation**
Second Treatise Chapter V establishes that labour mixed with nature creates property. This became the foundational argument for private property rights in liberal political theory. The argument has specific limits — the "Lockean proviso" (enough and as good left for others) — which Locke documented but which his followers largely ignored.
Source: Locke, Second Treatise §27–51; C.B. Macpherson, The Political Theory of Possessive Individualism (Oxford, 1962) for the contested reading.

**4. The Great Recoinage recommendation (1695–96)**
Locke served on the Board of Trade and submitted a documented policy recommendation on the recoinage of English silver currency. He argued for maintaining the silver standard at full face value against debasement. His position prevailed. The result was a deflationary shock.
Source: Locke, Further Considerations Concerning Raising the Value of Money (1695); Kelly, ed., Locke on Money, vol.2 (Clarendon, 1991); Board of Trade records.

**5. The Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina (1669)**
Locke drafted this governance document for the Lords Proprietors of Carolina. It established a feudal-style aristocracy and explicitly entrenched slavery. This directly contradicts his natural rights arguments and is not a minor inconsistency — it is a major governance document he authored.
Source: Locke, The Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina (1669), published in Locke: Political Essays, ed. Goldie (Cambridge, 1997); David Armitage, "John Locke, Carolina, and the Two Treatises of Government," Political Theory 32:5 (2004).

**6. An Essay Concerning Human Understanding — the tabula rasa argument (1689)**
Locke's epistemological claim that the mind is a blank slate at birth — all knowledge comes from experience — has profound political implications: human character is formed by environment and education, not nature. This underwrites his arguments for education reform and makes social arrangements, not human nature, responsible for inequality.
Source: Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689), Book II.

**7. Some Thoughts Concerning Education (1693)**
A practical treatise on education arguing that character is formed through habit and environment, not innate qualities. Documented positions on corporal punishment (against), Latin (conditional), physical exercise (for), and the development of reason as the goal of education.
Source: Locke, Some Thoughts Concerning Education (1693); critical edition by Yolton & Yolton (Clarendon, 1989).

**8. The Reasonableness of Christianity (1695)**
Locke argued that Christianity, properly understood, requires only that one accept Jesus as the Messiah — stripping away doctrinal accretions. This was controversial and brought accusations of Socinianism (a form of heresy). His decision to publish anonymously and his subsequent private defence of the work are documented.
Source: Locke, The Reasonableness of Christianity (1695); correspondence with Edward Stillingfleet, Bishop of Worcester, published in Locke's Works.

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Positions
*Secondary positions that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**Political counsel and private positions:**
- Private correspondence with Anthony Ashley Cooper (Earl of Shaftesbury) reveals Locke as a political operator — advising on parliamentary strategy and opposition tactics, not merely a theorist ✦ (Locke Correspondence vols.1–2)
- His approach to the Exclusion Crisis (1679–81): Locke supported excluding the Catholic James from succession — a constitutional crisis he helped theorise ✦
- Decision to flee to Holland 1683 after the Rye House Plot — suggests he was more directly involved in resistance planning than his published works admit ◈
- Private notes on toleration go further than his published Letter — suggest he personally favoured broader toleration than he publicly argued was politically achievable ◈ (Locke MS c28, Bodleian)

**Economic positions:**
- 1668 essay on interest rates: argued for lowering the legal interest rate ceiling — opposite of his 1692 position. Documented revision of economic views over 24 years ✦
- Monetary theory: documented belief that the value of money derives from agreement and convention, not intrinsic metal value — an early proto-nominalist position ◈
- Opposition to monopolies — consistent documented position in Board of Trade papers ✦
- Colonial trade: supported expanding Atlantic trade while on Board of Trade, including trade in enslaved people — documented in Board of Trade records 1696–1700 ✦

**Governance and institutions:**
- Board of Trade recommendations on Ireland: documented position that English economic interests should override Irish welfare — contradicts his natural rights framework ✦
- Consistent documented argument that representative assemblies must control taxation — no taxation without representation, decades before the American formulation ✦
- Private position on the monarchy: more republican in private correspondence than in published work; published work carefully preserved monarchy as compatible with consent theory ◈

## T3 — Stated Positions and Arguments
*Documented positions indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: ✦ unless noted*

### Government Legitimacy and Consent
- "The supreme power cannot take from any man any part of his property without his own consent." Second Treatise §138. ✦
- Government derives authority from the consent of the governed. Without consent, authority is mere force. Second Treatise §119–122. ✦
- Majority rule is the practical mechanism of consent — not because the majority is always right but because unanimity is unworkable. Second Treatise §96–98. ✦
- A government that systematically violates natural rights dissolves itself — the people do not rebel against it, they observe its self-dissolution. Second Treatise §212–222. ✦

### Property Rights
- Labour mixed with natural resources creates legitimate property. The Lockean proviso: this holds only when "enough and as good" remains for others. Second Treatise §27. ✦
- Money (as a non-perishable store of value agreed by convention) justifies accumulation beyond immediate need. Second Treatise §47–50. ✦
- Property rights precede government — government exists to protect them, not to create them. Second Treatise §123–131. ✦
- Documented tension: Locke never resolved whether the Lockean proviso still applies when resources are scarce. This is a genuine gap in his theory, acknowledged by scholars. ◈

### Religious Toleration
- The state has no authority over the soul — religious belief cannot be compelled by force. Letter Concerning Toleration. ✦
- Toleration is limited: it does not extend to those whose religion requires civil disloyalty (Catholics) or to those who deny the basis of moral obligation (atheists). Letter Concerning Toleration. ✦
- Documented position: toleration promotes social stability — religious persecution creates civil war, not religious conformity. Letter Concerning Toleration. ✦

### Education and Human Development
- Children are not born with innate ideas — they are formed by experience and education. Essay Concerning Human Understanding II.1. ✦
- The goal of education is not knowledge transmission but the development of reason and virtue. Some Thoughts §31–36. ✦
- Corporal punishment in education is counterproductive — it produces resentment, not compliance. Some Thoughts §47–52. ✦

### Economics and Property (from Board of Trade)
- Opposition to monopolies: documented in multiple Board of Trade submissions. ✦
- On the recoinage: maintaining silver standard at full value preferred over debasement. Further Considerations (1695). ✦
- On interest rates: position evolved — 1668 favoured lower caps; 1692 opposed lowering caps. Both documented. ✦

### Revolution and Resistance
- The right of revolution is not a right of individuals but of the people collectively — and only invoked when government has systematically destroyed the conditions of legitimate rule. Second Treatise §208–210. ✦
- "Wherever law ends, tyranny begins." Second Treatise §202. ✦
- Revolution is conservative, not radical — it restores the original compact, it does not create a new one. Second Treatise §243. ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Documented contradictions between stated positions. Confidence: ✦*

**1. Slavery: natural rights vs the Fundamental Constitutions**
Locke's Second Treatise argues that no man can consent to his own enslavement (§23) and that the state of slavery is a state of war. Simultaneously, he drafted the Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina which gave masters "absolute power and authority over his Negro slaves." He also held shares in the Royal African Company. No reconciliation of these positions is documented in his writings. He never addressed the contradiction.
Source: Second Treatise §23; Fundamental Constitutions §110; Wayne Glausser, "Three Approaches to Locke and the Slave Trade," Journal of the History of Ideas 51:2 (1990).

**2. Toleration: principle vs practice**
The Letter Concerning Toleration argues for broad religious toleration on principled grounds. Yet Locke explicitly excluded Catholics, atheists, and (implicitly) Muslims. His Board of Trade work supported the Atlantic slave trade. The principled argument and the practical exclusions are in direct tension.
Source: Letter Concerning Toleration; Board of Trade records; Jeremy Waldron, God, Locke, and Equality (Cambridge, 2002).

**3. Interest rates: 1668 vs 1692**
In 1668 Locke wrote an essay supporting lowering the legal ceiling on interest rates. In 1692's Some Considerations of the Consequences of the Lowering of Interest, he argued against lowering interest rates. The positions are opposite. The revision is documented but Locke never explicitly acknowledged or explained it.
Source: Locke, "Essay on Interest" (1668) in Political Essays ed. Goldie; Some Considerations (1692).

**4. Consent: theory vs colonial practice**
Locke's consent theory requires that legitimate government rests on the agreement of those governed. His Board of Trade work explicitly extended English governance to colonial populations without their consent. He never applied his consent framework to colonial subjects.
Source: Board of Trade records 1696–1700; Barbara Arneil, John Locke and America (Oxford, 1996).

**5. Property proviso: stated vs operationalised**
The Lockean proviso ("enough and as good left for others") is stated clearly in the Second Treatise. Locke then uses money and the convention of accumulation to effectively suspend the proviso without arguing that "enough and as good" remains. The logical tension is unresolved in his texts.
Source: Second Treatise §27–50; G.A. Cohen, Self-Ownership, Freedom and Equality (Cambridge, 1995) for the critique.

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Intellectual and practical failures with sources. Confidence: ✦*

**1. The Great Recoinage — economic harm (1696)**
Locke's recommendation to restore silver coinage at full face value prevailed. The result was a severe deflationary shock — the 1690s recoinage crisis. Credit contracted, trade suffered, and the English economy experienced significant hardship. Contemporaries including William Lowndes documented the harm.
Source: Lowndes, A Report Containing an Essay for the Amendment of the Silver Coins (1695); Ming-Hsun Li, The Great Recoinage of 1696 to 1699 (1963).

**2. Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina — governance failure**
The governance framework Locke drafted for Carolina was partially implemented and produced outcomes he never publicly acknowledged: a landed aristocracy, plantation slavery, and colonial exploitation. The document was formally abandoned by 1698 but its slave provisions shaped subsequent colonial governance.
Source: Historical record of colonial Carolina; Armitage (2004).

**3. The consent framework — inapplicability to existing populations**
Locke's consent theory was designed for a hypothetical state of nature. Applied to actual political situations, it provides no mechanism for populations already living under governments they did not choose. This theoretical failure was identified by contemporaries and became a major point of attack for Hume and Burke.
Source: David Hume, "Of the Original Contract" (1748); Edmund Burke, Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790).

**4. Exclusion of Catholics — political miscalculation**
Locke's exclusion of Catholics from toleration on grounds of political loyalty (to Rome) proved to be a category error: it conflated religious identity with political allegiance and created a principled exception that has been used to justify religious discrimination ever since.
Source: Letter Concerning Toleration; historical analysis in John Coffey, Persecution and Toleration in Protestant England (2000).

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Locke when the issue involves:**
- Legitimacy of government and consent — when is a government legitimate?
- Property rights, expropriation, and the state's authority over private assets
- Religious or ideological toleration and its limits
- The right to resist or replace a government
- Constitutionalism, separation of powers, rule of law
- Education policy and human development
- Any question about the foundational rights that governments may not override

**Do not select Locke when the issue primarily involves:**
- Geopolitics, military strategy, international relations (no framework)
- Economic policy specifics — monetary policy, fiscal policy, trade (thin and contradictory record)
- Crisis management (no operational framework)
- Social policy beyond education — healthcare, welfare, inequality (not addressed)
- Technology, AI, surveillance — extrapolation only, though natural rights framework is applicable if flagged ◇

**Interpretive note:**
Locke has been claimed by libertarians (property rights, limited government), liberals (consent, individual rights), and radicals (right of revolution, blank slate). When invoking Locke, specify which reading and cite the textual basis. The contradictions in T4 are essential context — particularly the slavery contradiction, which must not be omitted.
