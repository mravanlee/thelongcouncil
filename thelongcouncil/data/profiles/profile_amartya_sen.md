# Amartya Sen — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Economist and philosopher · India/United Kingdom/United States
Born: Santiniketan, West Bengal, 3 November 1933 · Living

---

## Doctrine Summary

Sen's intellectual contribution rests on three foundational arguments: the capability approach — that human development should be measured not by income or utility but by what people are actually able to do and be; that famines are not caused by food shortages but by failures of entitlement — the breakdown of people's ability to access food through their legitimate claims on the economy; and that democracy and public reasoning are essential instruments of development, not luxuries that come after economic growth. He won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1998. Unlike most economists on this council, Sen works at the intersection of economics, philosophy and political theory — his framework is explicitly normative, asking not just what policies produce growth but what makes development genuinely valuable. He is the most important non-Western voice in liberal development thought and provides the essential counter to both Deng's authoritarian development model and the pure GDP-growth frameworks of Friedman and Keynes.

## T1 — Intellectual Landmark Decisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. Poverty and Famines (1981)**
Sen's landmark work arguing that famines are not caused by absolute food shortages but by failures of entitlement — the collapse of people's ability to acquire food through trade, production, or transfer. The 1943 Bengal famine occurred while food was being exported from Bengal. This reframed famine from a natural disaster requiring food aid into a political failure requiring institutional reform.
Source: Sen, Poverty and Famines (Oxford University Press, 1981); documented application to Bengal 1943, Ethiopia 1972–74, Bangladesh 1974.

**2. The capability approach — Development as Freedom (1999)**
Sen's foundational reframing of development: the goal is not income growth but the expansion of human capabilities — the real freedoms people have to live lives they have reason to value. GDP per capita is an instrument, not an end. Five types of freedom are simultaneously means and ends: political freedoms, economic facilities, social opportunities, transparency guarantees, protective security.
Source: Sen, Development as Freedom (Oxford University Press, 1999); earlier formulation in "Equality of What?" (1979 Tanner Lecture).

**3. Collective Choice and Social Welfare (1970)**
Sen's first major work — a systematic treatment of social choice theory and Arrow's impossibility theorem. He showed that consistent collective decision-making is possible if individual preferences are allowed to be interpersonally comparable. A technical contribution that established his credibility as an economist before his philosophical work.
Source: Sen, Collective Choice and Social Welfare (Holden-Day, 1970; expanded edition 2017).

**4. "Democracy as a Universal Value" (1999)**
Sen's documented argument that democracy is not a Western value being imposed on non-Western cultures but a genuinely universal value — because public reasoning and accountability are necessary for development, famine prevention, and the protection of basic freedoms everywhere.
Source: Sen, "Democracy as a Universal Value," Journal of Democracy 10(3), 1999; extended in The Idea of Justice (2009).

**5. The Idea of Justice (2009)**
Sen's systematic critique of ideal justice theory (specifically Rawls) — arguing that the search for perfectly just institutions is less useful than the comparative evaluation of actual injustices. Justice is about removing manifest injustices, not designing perfect institutions from behind a veil of ignorance.
Source: Sen, The Idea of Justice (Harvard University Press, 2009).

**6. Identity and Violence (2006)**
Sen's argument against "civilisational" theories of conflict (Huntington's clash of civilisations) — that people have multiple, plural identities and that violence results from the political manipulation of singular identity, not from civilisational incompatibility.
Source: Sen, Identity and Violence: The Illusion of Destiny (W.W. Norton, 2006).

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Positions
*Confidence: [documented]/[inferred]*

**On gender and development:**
- Documented work on "missing women" — the 100+ million women absent from Asian and African populations due to sex-selective abortion, female infanticide, and differential access to food and healthcare [documented] (Sen, "More Than 100 Million Women Are Missing," New York Review of Books, 1990)
- Consistent documented position: women's education and autonomy are the most powerful instruments of development and fertility reduction [documented]

**On markets and the state:**
- Documented position: markets are powerful instruments that need to be complemented by social provision and public action — not alternatives to each other [documented] (Development as Freedom Chapter 5)
- Consistent documented critique of GDP as a development metric — it measures the wrong things [documented]
- Documented position: the Human Development Index (which Sen helped design for UNDP) is better than GDP but still incomplete [documented]

**On public reasoning:**
- Documented position: a free press is an instrument of development, not a luxury — it is the mechanism by which governments learn what is going wrong [documented] (no democracy has experienced a famine)
- Documented position: Rawls's veil of ignorance is too removed from actual human experience — justice requires impartial reasoning that includes the perspectives of those affected, including those outside the society being designed [documented] (The Idea of Justice)

## T3 — Stated Positions
*Indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: [documented] unless noted*

### Development and Capability
- "Development can be seen as a process of expanding the real freedoms that people enjoy." Development as Freedom Chapter 1. [documented]
- Documented position: income is instrumentally important but cannot be the metric of development — a person with high income but serious illness, disability or social exclusion is not well-developed. [documented]
- "The entitlement approach views starvation as a result of people not having enough food to eat, not as a result of there not being enough food." Poverty and Famines. [documented]
- Documented position: human capital theory (people as instruments of production) is importantly different from the capability approach (people as ends in themselves). [documented]

### Democracy and Public Reasoning
- "No substantial famine has ever occurred in any independent and democratic country with a relatively free press." Development as Freedom. [documented] — one of his most empirically specific and documented claims
- Documented position: democracy is not merely a political system but an epistemological instrument — it generates the information governments need to correct failures. [documented]
- "The demands of justice and democracy are not in tension with economic development — they are preconditions of it." Multiple documented sources. [inferred from documented framework]

### Justice and Inequality
- Documented critique of Rawls: the search for perfectly just institutions misses the point — we need to be able to compare actual states of affairs and identify manifest injustices, not design ideal institutions. [documented] (The Idea of Justice)
- Documented position: inequality matters not just instrumentally (because it reduces welfare) but intrinsically — some inequalities are unjust regardless of their aggregate effects. [documented]
- On gender: "More than 100 million women are missing." — the most powerful single statistic in the development literature on gender inequality. [documented]

### Markets and Institutions
- Documented position: the market is a mechanism with enormous power that requires complementary institutions — property rights, contract enforcement, social safety nets — to function well. [documented]
- Documented critique of the Washington Consensus: it focused on market liberalisation while ignoring the institutional preconditions for markets to work. [documented]

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The capability approach — what capabilities count?**
Sen deliberately left the capability approach incomplete — he refused to specify a definitive list of basic capabilities, arguing that this should be determined through democratic deliberation in each society. This has been criticised as making the framework too indeterminate for practical policy. Martha Nussbaum developed a specific list (the central capabilities) that Sen did not endorse. The tension between democratic determination of capabilities and universal standards is a documented unresolved issue.
Source: Sen vs Nussbaum debate; documented in Martha Nussbaum, Creating Capabilities (2011).

**2. The Idea of Justice vs Rawls**
Sen's critique of Rawls in The Idea of Justice is powerful but his own framework for "comparative justice" is less systematic. Critics have noted that comparative evaluations of injustice require some standard against which to compare — and Sen does not fully specify what that standard is.
Source: Documented in Samuel Freeman, "Amartya Sen's The Idea of Justice," Journal of Philosophy (2010).

**3. Democracy as universal value — empirical tensions**
Sen's claim that no democracy has experienced a famine is empirically well-documented. His broader claim that democracy is a universal value — not a Western imposition — has been challenged by documented cases of democratic backsliding in non-Western contexts. Sen has responded to these challenges but the tension between his universalism and democratic failure is ongoing.
Source: Multiple documented responses to the democracy-authoritarianism development debate.

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The capability approach — operationalisation**
Sen's capability approach has been influential in development theory but difficult to operationalise for policy. The Human Development Index — which Sen helped design — was a step forward but remains controversial as a measurement framework. Many practitioners find it too indeterminate for budgetary decisions.
Source: UNDP Human Development Reports; documented critique in Sabina Alkire's work on operationalisation.

**2. The famine analysis — limits of entitlement approach**
Sen's entitlement approach was groundbreaking but has been shown to be incomplete. The 1984 Ethiopian famine and some subsequent famines involved food shortages as well as entitlement failures. The approach was revised by his collaborators (Drèze and Sen) to incorporate food availability decline alongside entitlement failure.
Source: Jean Drèze and Amartya Sen, Hunger and Public Action (1989) — the documented revision.

**3. Limited direct policy influence in India**
Despite being the most prominent Indian development economist and a significant influence on international development frameworks, Sen's capability approach has had limited direct influence on Indian domestic policy. India's development path has followed GDP growth models rather than capability-centred approaches, with significant persistence of malnutrition, gender inequality, and human capability deficits that his framework identifies.
Source: Documented in Jean Drèze and Amartya Sen, An Uncertain Glory: India and Its Contradictions (2013).

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Sen when the issue involves:**
- Development — what does genuine human development look like beyond GDP?
- Famine, hunger, and the political economy of food security
- Democracy as an instrument of development, not just a value
- Gender equality and the relationship between women's freedom and development outcomes
- Inequality — when is it unjust, not merely inefficient?
- The limits of market mechanisms and the need for complementary institutions
- Any question about how to measure whether a society is developing well
- Healthcare, education and social provision as capability-enabling institutions

**Do not select Sen when the issue primarily involves:**
- Monetary policy or macroeconomic management (not his primary framework)
- Military or security strategy (not his domain)
- Geopolitics and great power competition (thin documented record)
- Crisis management requiring urgent operational decisions

**Critical pairing note:**
Sen is the essential counterweight to both Deng (authoritarian development) and Friedman (GDP-growth liberalism). Any session on development, inequality or the purpose of governance is incomplete without him. He represents the capability tradition — asking not "how much did the economy grow?" but "what were people actually able to do?"
