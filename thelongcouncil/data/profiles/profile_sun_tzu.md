# Sun Tzu — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Military Strategist · State of Wu, China
Born: c.544 BC · Died: c.496 BC (dates contested)

---

## Doctrine Summary

Sun Tzu is the author of *The Art of War* (*Bingfa*) — the most influential strategic text in human history, applied continuously for 2,500 years across military, diplomatic, political, and economic contexts. His framework is not primarily about war but about the conditions under which conflict can be avoided, managed, and resolved at minimum cost. His core documented convictions: that supreme excellence in strategy is to defeat the enemy without fighting — to achieve one's objectives through positioning, intelligence, and the management of conditions rather than direct confrontation; that all warfare is based on deception — appearing weak when strong, strong when weak, and managing the adversary's perception of reality as a primary strategic instrument; that knowledge of oneself and knowledge of the adversary are equally essential and separately insufficient; and that the best strategy exploits the adversary's own errors rather than relying on one's own strength. He differs from every other military thinker by treating intelligence, deception, and the management of information as more decisive than force. His framework is genuinely applicable beyond military contexts — to diplomacy, economic competition, political strategy, and organisational management — and has been explicitly applied by Chinese, Japanese, and Korean strategic planners in ways that are documented and analytically relevant.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*For a strategic philosopher, T1 captures the documented analytical framework and its influence. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. *The Art of War* — the strategic framework (c.500 BC)**
The thirteen chapters of *The Art of War* constitute the foundational documented text of strategic thought. Their authenticity as a coherent single-author text from the late Spring and Autumn period (c.500 BC) is supported by archaeological evidence — bamboo strip manuscripts discovered at Yinqueshan (1972) confirm the text's antiquity. The thirteen chapters address: strategic planning, waging war, strategic attack, tactical positioning, use of energy, weakness and strength, military manoeuvre, variation of tactics, on the march, terrain, the nine situations, attack by fire, and the use of intelligence.
Source: Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*; Samuel Griffith (trans.) (Oxford, 1963); Ralph Sawyer (trans.) (Westview, 1994); Yinqueshan Han Tomb archaeological report (1972).

**2. The demonstration to King Helu of Wu (c.512 BC)**
The documented biographical account of Sun Tzu's introduction to King Helu — in which he trained the king's concubines as soldiers, executed two of them when they disobeyed orders, and thereby demonstrated that command and discipline are not personal but institutional — is the most frequently cited documented case of his principles in practice. His documented position: command authority must be established clearly and maintained without personal sentiment.
Source: Sima Qian, *Shiji* (Records of the Grand Historian), "Sun Tzu Wu Qi Liezhuan" (Biographies of Sun Tzu and Wu Qi).

**3. The Wu-Yue campaigns — strategic application (c.512–496 BC)**
As a general for King Helu, Sun Tzu conducted campaigns against the states of Chu and Yue that are documented in the *Shiji* as applying his principles: attacking where the adversary is unprepared, moving swiftly to exploit confusion, using intelligence to identify vulnerabilities before engaging. These are the documented cases that validate the treatise.
Source: Sima Qian, *Shiji*; Edward Dreyer, "The *Art of War* in Context," introduction to Sawyer translation (1994).

**4. Documented influence on Chinese, Japanese, and Vietnamese strategic doctrine**
*The Art of War* was formally incorporated into Chinese military examinations from the Tang dynasty onward, translated into Japanese in the 8th century (influencing samurai strategy), and documented as a strategic reference by Vietnamese commanders in the 20th century conflicts. Vo Nguyen Giap explicitly acknowledged Sun Tzu's influence on his asymmetric warfare strategy against France and the United States.
Source: Benjamin Elman, *Civil Examinations and Meritocracy in Late Imperial China* (Harvard, 2013); Vo Nguyen Giap, documented interviews; Thomas Cleary, *The Japanese Art of War* (Shambhala, 1991).

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Secondary arguments that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**On intelligence:**
- Documented argument that no intelligence is more important than human intelligence — foreknowledge of adversary intentions and conditions cannot be obtained from spirits, experience, or calculation alone ✦ (*Art of War*, Chapter 13)
- Documented position that the wise ruler and capable commander use the most intelligent people as spies — intelligence is the highest-value investment in strategy ✦
- Documented taxonomy of intelligence agents: local agents, internal agents, converted agents, expendable agents, and living agents — a systematic intelligence architecture ✦

**On deception:**
- "All warfare is based on deception." *Art of War*, Chapter 1. ✦ — not an endorsement of dishonesty but a documented strategic principle: managing adversary perception is as important as managing one's own forces
- Documented argument that predictable strategy is vulnerable strategy — the adversary who knows your method can defeat it ✦

**On resource management:**
- Documented argument that prolonged war destroys the state even if it is won — "There is no instance of a nation benefiting from prolonged warfare." *Art of War*, Chapter 2. ✦
- Documented position that living off the adversary's resources — foraging in enemy territory — is more economically efficient than long supply chains ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On Strategy and Victory
- "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting." *Art of War*, Chapter 3. ✦ — the most cited and most misunderstood claim: direct confrontation is the most expensive form of strategy
- "Know the enemy and know yourself; in a hundred battles you will never be in peril." *Art of War*, Chapter 3. ✦
- Documented argument that the strategist who wins creates the conditions for victory before the battle; the strategist who loses fights first and seeks victory afterward ✦

### On Intelligence and Information
- Documented argument that the commander who does not use intelligence is not compassionate but incompetent — he wastes lives by fighting preventable battles ✦
- "All men can see these tactics whereby I conquer, but what none can see is the strategy out of which victory is evolved." *Art of War*, Chapter 6. ◈

### On Adaptation
- Documented argument that strategy must adapt to conditions — there are no fixed tactical methods, only fixed principles ✦ (*Art of War*, Chapter 6)
- "Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe he is facing." *Art of War*, Chapter 6. ✦

### On Leadership
- Documented argument that a general's five essential qualities are: wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage, and strictness — and that excess of any one is a danger ✦ (*Art of War*, Chapter 1)
- "If words of command are not clear and distinct, if orders are not thoroughly understood, the general is to blame." *Art of War*, Chapter 9. ✦

### On the Costs of Conflict
- Documented consistent position that war is expensive and destructive — the objective of strategy is to achieve goals at minimum cost, not to maximise the application of force ✦
- "The skillful leader subdues the enemy's troops without any fighting." ✦ — documented as the highest form of strategic achievement

## T4 — Contradictions and Tensions
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. Deception and trust**
Sun Tzu's documented framework treats deception as a primary strategic instrument. Applied to governance rather than military strategy, this creates a tension: a government that systematically manages the perception of its adversaries (foreign and domestic) undermines the trust-based legitimacy that Confucius and Ali ibn Abi Talib document as the foundation of durable governance. Sun Tzu does not address this boundary — his framework is for the commander, not the ruler. ◈

**2. Winning without fighting vs. the necessity of credible force**
Sun Tzu's documented preference for achieving objectives without direct confrontation requires that the adversary believes direct confrontation is possible and costly. A state that genuinely cannot fight is not practising Sun Tzu's strategy — it is simply weak. His framework requires credible force as the background condition for its preferred non-confrontational methods. ✦

**3. Authorship and text integrity**
The *Art of War*'s attribution to a single historical Sun Tzu (Sun Wu) is documented as contested. Some scholars argue it is a composite text from multiple authors across the Warring States period. The Yinqueshan manuscripts confirm the antiquity of the core text but do not resolve the authorship question. The council should apply ◈ to biographical claims and ✦ only to the textual positions. ◈

## T5 — Documented Failures and Limits
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. The framework is amoral**
*The Art of War* contains no framework for the justice or legitimacy of the objectives being pursued. It is purely instrumental — how to win, not whether winning is right. Applied to governance questions, this is a documented limit: the most sophisticated strategy in the service of unjust objectives is not good governance. ✦

**2. Not a governance framework**
Sun Tzu's framework is designed for military commanders and by extension strategic planners. Its application to economic policy, social governance, or institution-building requires substantial extrapolation. The council should flag ◇ when applying his framework beyond security and competitive strategy. ◈

**3. Asymmetric applicability**
Sun Tzu's framework is most applicable to situations of genuine strategic competition — great power rivalry, economic warfare, diplomatic negotiation under adversarial conditions. For cooperative governance problems (climate change, pandemic response, collective action), his adversarial framework is structurally inappropriate and may be actively counterproductive. ✦

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Sun Tzu when the issue involves:**
- Geopolitical competition and great power rivalry — US-China, regional security architecture
- Strategic positioning for small or medium states under pressure from larger ones
- Intelligence and information as governance instruments — not just military intelligence but the management of strategic knowledge
- Economic competition framed as strategic rivalry — trade wars, technology competition, sanctions
- Asymmetric strategy — how weaker actors achieve objectives against stronger adversaries
- Any question about deception, perception management, and strategic communication at state level

**Do not select Sun Tzu when the issue primarily involves:**
- Cooperative governance problems — climate, health, commons — his adversarial framework is counterproductive
- Domestic social or economic policy — not his domain
- Justice, rights, or legitimacy — his framework is amoral on these questions
- Democratic governance — not a category in his framework
