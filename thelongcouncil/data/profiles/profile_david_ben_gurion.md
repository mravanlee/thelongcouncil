# David Ben-Gurion — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Practitioner · Prime Minister of Israel 1948–53; 1955–63
Born: Płońsk, Russian Empire (modern Poland), 16 October 1886 · Died: Tel Aviv, 1 December 1973

---

## Doctrine Summary

David Ben-Gurion is the most complete documented case of simultaneous state-building and war-fighting in the 20th century — a leader who founded a state, fought a war of survival, built its institutions, and defined its strategic doctrine, all within the same compressed timeframe. His governing philosophy rests on a small number of documented convictions: that security is the precondition of everything else — a state without defensible borders cannot govern; that state-building and war-fighting must proceed simultaneously, not sequentially; that pragmatic alliances regardless of ideology are the instrument of survival for a small state with powerful enemies; and that the new state required a national culture — the *sabra* identity, Hebrew language, collective agricultural settlement — that was deliberately constructed, not inherited. He is analytically distinctive in the council for the specific combination: a small state surrounded by hostile larger states, with no natural resources, dependent on diaspora financial support, and required to build democratic institutions while fighting for survival. His framework is directly applicable to any question about state survival, security primacy, the relationship between military capability and diplomatic legitimacy, and the governance of a state whose existence is contested.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*Directly documented decisions traceable to specific moments and sources. Confidence: ✦*

**1. Declaration of Israeli independence (May 14, 1948)**
Ben-Gurion's decision to declare Israeli independence on May 14, 1948 — over the documented objections of the US State Department, which recommended delay, and against the advice of several of his own cabinet who feared immediate Arab invasion — is one of the most documented moments of political decision-making in the 20th century. His documented calculation: a window of international legitimacy existed; delay would close it; the military risk of immediate war was preferable to the political risk of indefinite postponement.
Source: Ben-Gurion's diary entries, May 12–14, 1948; Benny Morris, *1948: A History of the First Arab-Israeli War* (Yale, 2008); declassified US State Department cables.

**2. The Altalena Affair — establishing state monopoly on force (June 1948)**
When the Irgun (the rival Jewish paramilitary) attempted to import weapons independently of the new Israeli government, Ben-Gurion ordered the IDF to sink the Altalena arms ship — killing 16 Irgun fighters. His documented rationale: there can only be one army in a state; any armed force outside state command is a threat to the state itself. This is a documented exercise in establishing the state's monopoly on legitimate violence — Max Weber's definition of the state, applied in practice.
Source: Ben-Gurion's documented orders; Irgun records; Anita Shapira, *Ben-Gurion: Father of Modern Israel* (Yale, 2014) pp.134–142.

**3. The Law of Return and the ingathering of exiles (1950)**
Ben-Gurion's Law of Return — granting every Jewish person the right to immigrate to Israel — is the foundational documented statement of Israeli state identity. Combined with his documented policy of mass immigration from North Africa, the Middle East, and Europe, it transformed Israel from a largely Ashkenazi settler society to a multiethnic Jewish state within a decade.
Source: Law of Return (1950); documented immigration statistics; Tom Segev, *1949: The First Israelis* (Free Press, 1986).

**4. The Sinai Campaign — alliance with France and Britain (1956)**
Ben-Gurion's documented decision to participate in the Anglo-French Suez operation against Egypt — coordinated secretly at Sèvres — secured French military equipment and temporarily destroyed Egypt's military capability. His documented calculation: the strategic window provided by Anglo-French interests aligned with Israel's security need. The subsequent US pressure to withdraw was a documented lesson in the limits of great power alliances for small states.
Source: The Sèvres Protocol (October 22–24, 1956, declassified); Shimon Peres's documented role; Avi Shlaim, *The Iron Wall* (Norton, 2000).

**5. The Eichmann trial — documented use of justice as political education (1961)**
Ben-Gurion's documented decision to pursue the capture and trial of Adolf Eichmann in Argentina — and to insist the trial be held in Jerusalem, not at an international tribunal — was simultaneously a documented act of justice and a documented act of political communication: the Holocaust was not merely a European tragedy but specifically a Jewish tragedy that only a Jewish state could avenge and memorialize.
Source: Ben-Gurion's documented correspondence on the Eichmann capture; Hannah Arendt's documented critique; Deborah Lipstadt, *The Eichmann Trial* (Schocken, 2011).

**6. Nuclear development — Dimona (late 1950s)**
Ben-Gurion's documented decision to pursue nuclear capability — the Dimona reactor, constructed with French assistance and maintained under a policy of "nuclear ambiguity" — is one of the most significant documented security decisions in the council's practitioner roster. His documented rationale: a small state surrounded by hostile larger states cannot rely solely on conventional military deterrence.
Source: Avner Cohen, *Israel and the Bomb* (Columbia, 1998); declassified US intelligence assessments.

*Secondary decisions that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**Security and military:**
- Documented insistence on universal military service — building a citizen army rather than a professional one ✦
- Documented decision to develop an independent military-industrial base rather than depend on arms imports alone ✦
- Documented doctrine of pre-emptive strike as a legitimate instrument when threat is sufficiently documented — established the principle that was applied in 1967 ✦

**Institution-building:**
- Documented decision to make Hebrew the sole official language of the state — requiring mass adult language acquisition ✦
- Documented dissolution of the Palmach (the elite Haganah striking force) to prevent a separate military command structure ✦

- Documented "peripheral strategy" — alliances with non-Arab states on the periphery of the Arab world (Iran, Turkey, Ethiopia) as a strategic counterweight ✦
- Documented pragmatic relationship with West Germany — including reparations agreement (1952) against intense domestic opposition — for strategic and economic reasons ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On Security and Survival
- "Security is the precondition of everything — a state without defensible borders cannot govern." ◈ — consistent documented position across his entire tenure
- Documented argument that small states surrounded by hostile neighbours cannot afford the luxury of reactive security policy — initiative and pre-emption are survival requirements ✦
- "State-building and war-fighting must proceed simultaneously — you cannot wait for peace to build institutions." ◈ — the documented lesson of 1948

### On Pragmatic Alliances
- "Pragmatic alliances regardless of ideology — survival trumps political purity." ◈ — documented across the French alliance, German reparations, and peripheral strategy
- Documented argument that Israel's survival required making itself useful to great powers — not merely making moral claims on them ✦
- Documented rejection of reliance on any single great power protector — the Suez aftermath reinforced documented scepticism about US reliability ✦

### On State Identity
- Documented argument that a new state requires the deliberate construction of national culture — it cannot be merely inherited from diaspora traditions ✦
- Consistent documented position that the Hebrew language was the indispensable instrument of national cohesion ✦
- Documented *mamlachtiut* (statism) — the state as the highest legitimate authority, superseding party, religious, and communal loyalties ✦

### On Democracy and Security
- Documented consistent position that democratic governance and security imperatives are not in conflict but that security must be the first priority in an existential threat environment ✦
- Documented decision to accept UN partition plan (1947) as the pragmatic path to statehood — documented against the maximalist position ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Tensions
*Documented position changes. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. Democracy and security in tension**
Ben-Gurion maintained democratic institutions while simultaneously imposing military governance on Arab citizens (Military Administration 1948–66), using Shin Bet for domestic political surveillance, and concentrating security decision-making in a small circle. His documented position — that existential threat justified security measures that would be unacceptable in a non-threatened democracy — is the documented source of Israel's unresolved tension between security and civil liberties. ✦

**2. German reparations — principle vs. pragmatism**
Ben-Gurion's documented decision to negotiate reparations with West Germany (1952) — over the documented intense opposition of Holocaust survivors and opposition parties including Begin's Herut — is his clearest documented exercise of pragmatic calculation over moral position. His documented argument: Israel needed the resources; moral purity did not feed people. ✦

**3. Nuclear ambiguity vs. transparent security doctrine**
Ben-Gurion's documented nuclear strategy — pursue capability, deny its existence — created a deliberate ambiguity that served strategic purposes but documented undermined the transparency that democratic governance normally requires. ✦

## T5 — Documented Failures and Limits
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. The Arab citizens of Israel — military administration**
Ben-Gurion's documented Military Administration over Arab citizens of Israel (1948–66) subjected them to movement restrictions, land expropriations, and military governance for eighteen years. This is a documented failure to extend the democratic principles of the state equally to all its citizens. ✦

**2. The ethnic gap — Mizrahi Jews**
The mass immigration of Jews from Arab countries under Ben-Gurion's Law of Return was accompanied by documented cultural condescension — the assumption that Ashkenazi cultural norms were the standard for the new Israeli identity. The documented economic and social gap between Ashkenazi and Mizrahi communities that developed during his tenure has structured Israeli politics for sixty years. ✦

**3. Suez — miscalculation of US response**
Ben-Gurion's documented calculation that the US would accept the Suez operation was wrong. Eisenhower's documented pressure produced a humiliating withdrawal. The strategic lesson — that Israel's alliance with the US was the essential anchor — was absorbed after the fact, not anticipated. ✦

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Ben-Gurion when the issue involves:**
- Small state survival under existential threat — the security-first governance model
- Simultaneous state-building and conflict — when institution-building cannot wait for peace
- Nuclear deterrence for small states — the case for nuclear ambiguity
- Pragmatic alliances — when and how to prioritise survival over ideological alignment
- National identity construction — the deliberate building of a new national culture
- Security vs. democratic norms — the documented tension between existential threat and civil liberties
- Middle East specifically — any question where Israeli strategic experience is directly relevant

**Do not select Ben-Gurion when the issue primarily involves:**
- Economic development — his framework prioritises security over economic policy
- Social equality — his record on Arab citizens and Mizrahi Jews is the counterargument
- Post-conflict reconciliation — his framework is not designed for this
- Technology governance — structural extrapolation required; flag as ◇
