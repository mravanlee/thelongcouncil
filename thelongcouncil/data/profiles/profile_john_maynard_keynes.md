# John Maynard Keynes — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Economist, civil servant, diplomat · United Kingdom
Born: Cambridge, 5 June 1883 · Died: Firle, East Sussex, 21 April 1946

---

## Doctrine Summary

Keynes's contribution to governance thinking rests on three foundational arguments: that markets left to themselves do not automatically clear — unemployment and stagnation can be stable equilibria requiring active government intervention; that under genuine uncertainty (as distinct from calculable risk) the correct policy is not to optimise for the most likely outcome but to insure against the worst plausible one; and that the long run is a poor guide to policy because "in the long run we are all dead." He was the most influential economist of the 20th century and the intellectual architect of the post-war international economic order (Bretton Woods). Unlike most Framers, Keynes was also a practitioner of consequence — he served as a senior Treasury official, led the British delegation at Versailles, and negotiated the Bretton Woods settlement. His record includes both foundational intellectual decisions and documented practical interventions with traceable consequences.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions and Intellectual Interventions
*Confidence: ✦*

**1. The Economic Consequences of the Peace (1919)**
Keynes resigned from the British delegation at the Paris Peace Conference and published a devastating critique of the Versailles reparations settlement. He argued — correctly, as events proved — that the reparations demanded of Germany were economically unworkable and would produce political catastrophe. This was not an academic paper; it was a public intervention with direct political consequences, and it made him internationally famous.
Source: Keynes, The Economic Consequences of the Peace (1919); Robert Skidelsky, John Maynard Keynes vol.1 (Macmillan, 1983) pp.371–401.

**2. Opposition to returning to the Gold Standard (1925)**
Keynes publicly opposed Churchill's decision to return sterling to the Gold Standard at pre-war parity. His pamphlet The Economic Consequences of Mr Churchill (1925) argued the decision would require wage deflation, unemployment and industrial conflict. He was correct: the 1926 General Strike followed. This is a documented prediction that proved accurate.
Source: Keynes, The Economic Consequences of Mr Churchill (1925); Skidelsky vol.2 pp.196–211.

**3. The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936)**
Keynes's foundational intellectual decision: to argue that the prevailing economic orthodoxy (Say's Law — supply creates its own demand) was wrong and that economies could reach stable equilibria with significant unemployment. The General Theory established the intellectual basis for counter-cyclical fiscal policy and active demand management.
Source: Keynes, The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936); analysis in Hyman Minsky, John Maynard Keynes (Columbia, 1975).

**4. The Bretton Woods proposal — Bancor plan (1944)**
At the Bretton Woods conference, Keynes proposed an International Clearing Union with a new international currency (Bancor) that would impose symmetric adjustment obligations on both surplus and deficit countries. The American delegation — led by Harry Dexter White — rejected it in favour of a dollar-centred system. Keynes's proposal was intellectually superior; it lost because Britain was the weaker power. The asymmetry he warned against became the structural problem of the post-war monetary order.
Source: Keynes, "Proposals for an International Clearing Union" (1943), published in Collected Writings vol.25; Benn Steil, The Battle of Bretton Woods (Princeton, 2013).

**5. The 1941 US Loan negotiations**
Keynes led the British delegation negotiating the post-war American loan that kept Britain solvent. He secured $3.75 billion but at conditions — convertibility of sterling within a year — that he considered too harsh and that he privately predicted would be unworkable. He was right; the convertibility crisis of 1947 followed his death.
Source: Keynes, Collected Writings vol.24; Skidelsky vol.3 pp.403–465.

**6. How to Pay for the War (1940)**
Keynes proposed compulsory savings as the mechanism for financing the war without inflation — a specific, practical policy document that directly influenced British wartime economic management. It demonstrated his ability to translate theoretical framework into operational policy.
Source: Keynes, How to Pay for the War (1940); Collected Writings vol.22.

**7. The distinction between risk and uncertainty (Treatise on Probability, 1921)**
Keynes's philosophical contribution: the distinction between situations where probabilities can be calculated (risk) and situations where they cannot (genuine uncertainty). This is the intellectual foundation for his argument that under uncertainty, conventional economic optimisation is inappropriate — the correct response is insurance against worst cases, not optimisation for expected values.
Source: Keynes, A Treatise on Probability (1921); analysis in Rod O'Donnell, Keynes: Philosophy, Economics and Politics (Macmillan, 1989).

**8. Memoranda on Indian Currency and Finance (1913)**
Keynes's first major intellectual intervention was on Indian monetary policy — he served on the Royal Commission on Indian Finance and Currency. His analysis of the gold-exchange standard and currency management in colonial contexts showed his analytical framework before the General Theory.
Source: Keynes, Indian Currency and Finance (1913); Collected Writings vol.1.

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**Intellectual revisions:**
- Keynes's evolution from quantity theory of money to liquidity preference theory — documented in correspondence with Dennis Robertson 1930–35 ✦
- His private acknowledgment that the General Theory needed further development — correspondence with Joan Robinson and Richard Kahn ✦
- Revision of his views on trade protection — initially a free trader, moved toward qualified protectionism in the 1930s, documented in collected writings ✦

**Practical interventions:**
- His management of King's College Cambridge endowment — turned away from conventional portfolio theory toward equity concentration, documented in college records, and outperformed the market significantly ✦
- His role in founding the Arts Council — documented cultural policy intervention ✦
- His advocacy for the National Health Service — documented support in wartime policy discussions ◈
- Bloomsbury Group membership — his intellectual formation was aesthetic and philosophical before it was economic; documented influence on his thinking about uncertainty and convention ◈

**Policy advocacy:**
- Support for public works programmes in the early 1930s — direct documented advocacy to the MacDonald government ✦
- Opposition to means-testing in unemployment policy — documented in submissions to the Macmillan Committee on Finance and Industry (1930) ✦
- Advocacy for lower interest rates as a policy tool — consistent documented position from 1930 onwards ✦

## T3 — Stated Positions and Arguments
*Indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: ✦ unless noted*

### Economic Crisis Management
- "The boom, not the slump, is the right time for austerity at the Treasury." The Times, 1937. ✦
- "In the long run we are all dead. Economists set themselves too easy, too useless a task if in tempestuous seasons they can only tell us that when the storm is long past the ocean is flat again." A Tract on Monetary Reform (1923). ✦
- Counter-cyclical fiscal policy: governments should deficit spend in recessions and run surpluses in booms. General Theory Chapter 22. ✦
- On austerity in a recession: "The more [money] the government spends, the less goes to private business. But in a slump the opposite is true." Documented in multiple sources 1930s. ✦
- "Nothing is more dangerous than an international banker." Documented scepticism of financial sector as economic driver. ◈

### Uncertainty and Decision-Making Under Uncertainty
- The most famous and applicable: "It is better to be roughly right than precisely wrong." ✦ (attributed, consistent with documented positions)
- "When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?" ✦ (attributed, consistent with documented pattern of revision)
- Under genuine uncertainty — where probabilities cannot be assigned — the correct policy is to insure against the worst plausible outcome, not to optimise for the expected outcome. Treatise on Probability; General Theory Chapter 12. ✦
- "Markets can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent." ✦ (attributed, consistent with documented investment philosophy)
- Animal spirits: investment decisions are driven by confidence and convention, not rational calculation. General Theory Chapter 12. ✦

### International Economic Order
- Symmetric adjustment: both surplus and deficit countries must bear adjustment costs in international monetary arrangements. Bancor proposal (1943). ✦
- "If nations can learn to provide themselves with full employment by their domestic policy... there need be no important economic forces calculated to set the interest of one country against that of its neighbours." General Theory Chapter 24. ✦
- Documented opposition to using international financial institutions as instruments of political conditionality — Bretton Woods negotiations. ✦
- "The IMF should never be allowed to become the instrument of national policy." ◈ (consistent with documented positions but not a single sourced quote)

### Fiscal Policy and Government Role
- Government has a legitimate role in managing aggregate demand — this was the radical claim of the General Theory. ✦
- The paradox of thrift: individually rational saving behaviour produces collectively irrational outcomes in a recession. General Theory Chapter 23. ✦
- Public works as economic stimulus: documented advocacy throughout the 1930s. ✦
- On debt: documented position that public debt in one's own currency is not equivalent to private debt — the government cannot go bankrupt in its own currency. ◈

### Financial Markets and Speculation
- "Speculators may do no harm as bubbles on a steady stream of enterprise. But the position is serious when enterprise becomes the bubble on a whirlpool of speculation." General Theory Chapter 12. ✦
- Financial markets as beauty contests: investors do not assess fundamental value but try to predict what other investors will value. General Theory Chapter 12. ✦
- Documented support for financial transaction taxes to reduce speculation — Keynes was an early advocate of what became the Tobin tax. ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: ✦*

**1. Free trade to qualified protectionism**
Keynes was a convinced free trader until the early 1930s. During the Depression he moved toward qualified protectionism, arguing that in conditions of mass unemployment, trade protection could preserve domestic employment even at the cost of efficiency. His 1933 essay "National Self-Sufficiency" explicitly advocates economic nationalism. This contradicts his earlier published positions.
Source: Keynes, "National Self-Sufficiency," Yale Review (1933); compare with his pre-1930 writings; Skidelsky vol.2 pp.476–489.

**2. Quantity theory to liquidity preference**
Keynes's early work (Indian Currency and Finance, Tract on Monetary Reform) operated within the quantity theory of money framework. The General Theory replaces this with liquidity preference — a fundamentally different mechanism. He never publicly acknowledged the extent of the break.
Source: Comparison of Tract on Monetary Reform (1923) with General Theory (1936); documented in Patinkin, Money, Interest and Prices (1956).

**3. Gold standard: complex to critic**
Keynes's 1913 work on Indian currency was largely supportive of the gold-exchange standard as a mechanism for monetary stability. His 1925 critique of the Gold Standard and his subsequent advocacy for managed currencies represents a significant revision — though arguably consistent with a deeper principle (monetary stability matters; the gold standard became an obstacle to it).
Source: Indian Currency and Finance (1913); Economic Consequences of Mr Churchill (1925); Treatise on Money (1930).

**4. The Bancor and dollar hegemony**
Keynes publicly advocated the Bancor plan and the International Clearing Union as the correct international monetary framework. When the American position prevailed, he publicly supported the Bretton Woods agreement he had failed to shape. His private reservations — that the dollar-centred system would be unstable — are documented in correspondence but not in his public statements after Bretton Woods.
Source: Keynes, Collected Writings vol.26; private correspondence with Lionel Robbins.

**5. Population and eugenics**
Keynes was a member of the British Eugenics Society and expressed eugenic views on population policy that are directly contradicted by his later emphasis on full employment for all workers. He never reconciled these positions. This is not a minor biographical footnote — it represents a documented contradiction between his economic inclusivity and his demographic views.
Source: Keynes, Eugenics Society membership records; documented speeches to the Eugenics Society 1937.

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: ✦*

**1. The 1941 American loan — convertibility condition**
Keynes secured the post-war American loan but accepted a convertibility clause — sterling must be made freely convertible within a year — that he privately believed was unworkable. He accepted it because Britain had no alternative. The 1947 convertibility crisis followed exactly as he had predicted privately. The failure was not his prediction but his inability to negotiate better terms.
Source: Keynes, Collected Writings vol.24; convertibility crisis documentation in Bank of England records.

**2. The Versailles failure — reparations implemented anyway**
The Economic Consequences of the Peace was intellectually correct and politically unsuccessful. The reparations were implemented. Keynes's public campaign changed the intellectual climate but not the immediate policy outcome. The consequences he predicted — German political instability — followed.
Source: Historical record; Skidelsky vol.1 pp.398–420.

**3. The Bancor failure**
Keynes's most ambitious practical proposal — the International Clearing Union — was defeated by the Americans at Bretton Woods. The dollar-centred system that replaced it produced exactly the asymmetries he had warned against: surplus countries (Germany, Japan, China) face no obligation to adjust, creating persistent global imbalances. His framework was vindicated in retrospect; the proposal failed in practice.
Source: Steil (2013); IMF historical records.

**4. Eugenics — documented intellectual failure**
Keynes's eugenic views represent a documented case where his analytical framework — the importance of improving human welfare — was applied through a scientific lens (eugenics) that has since been thoroughly discredited. He was not unusual among his class and era, but the position is documented and cannot be omitted.
Source: Eugenics Society records; documented in Donald Moggridge, Maynard Keynes: An Economist's Biography (Routledge, 1992).

**5. The limits of the General Theory in open economies**
The General Theory was primarily designed for a closed economy. Its policy prescriptions — deficit spending, demand management — work differently in small open economies where stimulus leaks into imports. This theoretical limit was identified by Keynes's contemporaries and has been extensively documented.
Source: Marcus Fleming and Robert Mundell's subsequent work; academic literature on the Mundell-Fleming model.

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Keynes when the issue involves:**
- Economic recession, crisis management, fiscal response to downturns
- Uncertainty — when probabilities cannot be assigned to future scenarios
- International monetary architecture — exchange rates, capital flows, reserve currencies
- The role of government in economic management
- Financial market instability and speculation
- Insurance logic — when the worst plausible scenario matters more than the expected scenario
- Debt, deficits, and the difference between cyclical and structural fiscal positions
- Any question about whether austerity or stimulus is the correct response

**Do not select Keynes when the issue primarily involves:**
- Political governance, legitimacy, or institutional design (not his framework)
- Military or security strategy (no documented framework)
- Long-term structural development economics — his framework is cyclical, not developmental
- Social policy specifics beyond employment (thin record)
- Technology and AI — no documented positions

**Interpretive note:**
Keynes has been claimed by left (government intervention, full employment) and right (he was not anti-market, supported private enterprise). "Keynesian" is now used to mean almost anything involving government economic intervention. When invoking Keynes, specify which aspect of his framework is being applied: the uncertainty argument, the counter-cyclical argument, the international monetary architecture argument, or the animal spirits / market instability argument. These are distinct claims with distinct evidence bases.
