# Raúl Prebisch — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Economist · Executive Secretary, UN Economic Commission for Latin America (ECLAC) 1950–63; Secretary-General, UNCTAD 1964–69
Born: Tucumán, Argentina, 17 April 1901 · Died: Santiago, Chile, 29 April 1986

---

## Doctrine Summary

Raúl Prebisch is the founder of dependency theory and the architect of the intellectual case that the global trading system is structurally biased against developing countries. His core documented claim — the Prebisch-Singer hypothesis — is that the terms of trade between primary commodity exporters (the periphery) and manufactured goods exporters (the centre) systematically deteriorate over time: commodity prices fall relative to manufactured goods prices, transferring real income from poor countries to rich ones through the structure of trade itself. This was not a theoretical proposition derived from first principles but an empirical finding based on his analysis of British trade statistics from 1876 to 1947. The policy conclusion he drew — that developing countries must industrialise behind protective tariffs rather than specialising in primary commodities as comparative advantage theory recommended — shaped Latin American economic policy for thirty years and the entire UNCTAD development agenda. He is the indispensable voice whenever the council addresses the fairness of the international economic order, trade policy for developing countries, or the structural causes of persistent poverty. His framework directly and explicitly challenges Friedman, Hayek, and the free trade consensus — not on ideological grounds but on empirical ones.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*For an economist-policymaker, T1 captures documented analytical contributions and policy interventions. Confidence: ✦*

**1. The Prebisch-Singer hypothesis — the deteriorating terms of trade (1950)**
Prebisch's 1950 ECLAC report, *The Economic Development of Latin America and Its Principal Problems*, documented that the terms of trade between primary commodity exporters and manufactured goods exporters had deteriorated systematically over seven decades. He and Hans Singer reached the same conclusion independently. This is the foundational documented empirical claim of development economics as a distinct discipline.
Source: Raúl Prebisch, *The Economic Development of Latin America and Its Principal Problems* (ECLAC, 1950); Hans Singer, "The Distribution of Gains between Investing and Borrowing Countries," *American Economic Review* (1950).

**2. Import Substitution Industrialisation as policy framework (1950–63)**
Prebisch developed ISI — industrial development behind protective tariffs, state investment in manufacturing, and managed trade — as the policy response to deteriorating terms of trade. As ECLAC executive secretary, he made ECLAC the intellectual centre of a regional development strategy that shaped the economic policy of every major Latin American government between 1950 and 1980.
Source: ECLAC annual reports 1950–63; Joseph Love, *Crafting the Third World: Theorizing Underdevelopment in Rumania and Brazil* (Stanford, 1996).

**3. UNCTAD founding and the Group of 77 (1964)**
Prebisch was the founding Secretary-General of UNCTAD — the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development — which he designed as a counterweight to GATT, whose rules he documented as systematically favouring industrial countries. His inaugural report to UNCTAD I (Geneva, 1964) is the most comprehensive documented statement of the structural case for reforming the international trading system.
Source: Raúl Prebisch, *Towards a New Trade Policy for Development* (UNCTAD, 1964); Branislav Gosovic, *UNCTAD: Conflict and Compromise* (Sijthoff, 1972).

**4. Direction of the Central Bank of Argentina during the 1930s depression (1935–43)**
Before his international career, Prebisch ran Argentina's Central Bank during the Depression and the Second World War — managing exchange controls, debt renegotiation with Britain, and counter-cyclical fiscal policy. This is documented practical experience of the kind of crisis that shaped his later theoretical framework.
Source: Central Bank of Argentina records; Pablo Gerchunoff and Lucas Llach, *El ciclo de la ilusión y el desencanto* (Ariel, 1998).

**5. The New International Economic Order — intellectual architecture (1970s)**
Prebisch's framework was the intellectual foundation of the developing world's push for a New International Economic Order at the UN in the 1970s — including commodity price stabilisation, technology transfer on preferential terms, and reform of the Bretton Woods institutions. His documented position: the existing order was not neutral but structurally reproduced poverty.
Source: Prebisch, *Change and Development: Latin America's Great Task* (IDB, 1970); UN General Assembly Resolution 3201 (NIEO, 1974).

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Secondary arguments that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**Economic policy:**
- Documented argument for regional integration as a way to create markets large enough for industrial-scale production — the basis for the Latin American Free Trade Association (LAFTA, 1960) ✦
- Documented position that foreign direct investment in developing countries is not neutral — it transfers profits to the centre and can reinforce peripheral specialisation ✦
- Documented support for state-owned enterprises in strategic industries where private investment would not enter ✦
- Later documented qualification: ISI had created protected inefficient industries and needed reform — he was not a static thinker ✦

**On the international system:**
- Documented argument that GATT's reciprocity principle — equal treatment for structurally unequal partners — was itself a form of structural bias ✦
- Documented advocacy for commodity price stabilisation agreements as a mechanism to reduce terms-of-trade volatility ✦
- Documented position that technology transfer from developed to developing countries was a necessary condition for closing the productivity gap ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On Trade and Development
- Documented core claim: free trade between structurally unequal partners produces divergence, not convergence — Ricardo's comparative advantage theory assumes a static world that does not exist ✦
- "The advantages of technical progress have not been shared equitably between the centre and the periphery." *The Economic Development of Latin America* (1950). ✦
- Documented argument that industrial countries protect their own agriculture and manufacturing while insisting developing countries adopt free trade — the rules are applied asymmetrically ✦

### On Industrialisation
- Documented argument that no country has developed to high income status by specialising in primary commodities — industrialisation is the documented path ✦
- Documented position that infant industry protection is not a distortion but a corrective for the structural disadvantage facing new industries competing against established ones ✦
- Later documented qualification: protection must be temporary and conditional on performance — permanent protection produces the inefficiency he observed in Latin American ISI ✦

### On the International Order
- Documented argument that the Bretton Woods system — IMF, World Bank, GATT — was designed by and for industrial countries and its rules systematically disadvantaged developing ones ✦
- "Destroying domestic production voluntarily is not free trade — it is structural dependency." ◈ — consistent documented pattern across his corpus
- Documented position that the developing world needed its own institutional voice — the rationale for UNCTAD ✦

### On Latin America Specifically
- Documented diagnosis of Latin America's structural problem: export revenues from commodities are insufficient to finance the technology imports required for industrialisation ✦
- Documented argument that income inequality in Latin America reduces domestic demand and makes industrialisation harder — distribution and growth are connected, not in tension ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. ISI advocacy to ISI critique**
Prebisch spent the 1950s advocating import substitution industrialisation. By the late 1960s he was documenting its failures: protected industries had become permanently dependent on protection, income concentration had limited domestic markets, and the foreign exchange constraint had not been resolved. His 1970 report *Change and Development* is a documented self-critique of the policy he had designed. ✦

**2. State intervention and efficiency**
Prebisch documented the structural case for state-led industrialisation while also documenting the efficiency failures of state enterprises in practice. He never fully resolved the tension — his later writing acknowledged both the structural necessity and the practical failure of the state development model. ◈

**3. Terms of trade — empirical claim vs. long-run trend**
The Prebisch-Singer hypothesis has been subject to sustained empirical challenge. The terms of trade data show high volatility but not a consistent long-run deterioration across all commodities and all periods. Prebisch maintained the claim but acknowledged that the pattern was less uniform than his original formulation suggested. ◈

## T5 — Documented Failures and Limits
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

**1. ISI and the Latin American debt crisis**
The import substitution industrialisation strategy Prebisch designed contributed to the conditions that produced the Latin American debt crisis of the 1980s: protected industries required imported capital goods and technology, generating chronic foreign exchange deficits that were financed by external borrowing. Prebisch documented this failure honestly in his late work. ✦

**2. UNCTAD's limited impact**
UNCTAD under Prebisch produced important intellectual work but achieved limited concrete reform of the international trading system. The commodity price stabilisation agreements and preferential tariff schemes it negotiated were partial, contested, and largely dismantled by the 1980s. The structural reforms he sought were not achieved in his lifetime. ✦

**3. Regional integration — limited results**
Prebisch's advocacy for Latin American regional integration as a solution to small market size produced LAFTA (1960), which achieved limited trade liberalisation and did not produce the industrial-scale markets he had envisioned. The political fragmentation of Latin America that Bolívar had identified 150 years earlier remained the binding constraint. ✦

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Prebisch when the issue involves:**
- International trade architecture — whether the rules of global trade are neutral or structurally biased
- Development strategy for commodity-dependent economies
- Industrial policy — the case for infant industry protection vs. free trade
- The NIEO debate — developing country demands for reformed international economic institutions
- Latin American economic policy specifically
- Any question where Friedman or Hayek's free trade position needs a structurally grounded empirical challenge
- Commodity price volatility and its consequences for development finance

**Do not select Prebisch when the issue primarily involves:**
- Political governance — his framework is economic and institutional, not political
- Post-conflict reconstruction — not his domain
- Social policy — his framework addresses structural economics, not welfare design
- Technology governance — no documented record; flag as ◇
