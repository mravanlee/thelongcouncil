# Niccolò Machiavelli — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Political theorist and diplomat · Florence, Italian Peninsula
Born: Florence, 3 May 1469 · Died: Florence, 21 June 1527

---

## Doctrine Summary

Machiavelli's contribution to political thought rests on one foundational move: the separation of political analysis from moral prescription. He described how power actually works — not how it should work — and drew conclusions from that description. His central arguments: a ruler must be willing to use force, deception and fear when necessary, because a ruler who is only virtuous will be destroyed by those who are not; that political stability requires the appearance of virtue even when the substance is absent; that new orders are harder to establish than old ones because the beneficiaries of the new order are uncertain while the defenders of the old order are committed; and that fortune can be mastered by the energetic leader who meets it with force rather than prudence. He was not an advocate of evil — he was an analyst of power who believed that understanding reality was a precondition of acting effectively within it. His work is primary text, fully preserved, in Italian with authoritative translations. He also served as a practitioner — Florentine Chancellor and diplomatic envoy — and his reports from diplomatic missions are documented primary sources of extraordinary quality.

## T1 — Intellectual Landmark Decisions and Diplomatic Interventions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The Prince (written 1513, published 1532)**
Machiavelli's most famous work — a practical manual for acquiring and maintaining political power, dedicated to Lorenzo de' Medici. The central argument: a prince must learn to use both law (the human way) and force (the beast's way), and must combine the strength of the lion with the cunning of the fox. He must know how not to be good, and use this knowledge or not use it according to necessity.
Source: Machiavelli, The Prince (1513/1532); standard translations: Harvey Mansfield (Chicago, 1998); George Bull (Penguin, 1961).

**2. Discourses on Livy (written c.1517, published 1531)**
Machiavelli's longer and arguably more important work — a commentary on the first ten books of Livy's history of Rome, and his fullest statement of republican political theory. He argues that republics are more durable than principalities because they can adapt to changing circumstances by drawing on a wider range of citizens' abilities.
Source: Machiavelli, Discourses on Livy; standard translation: Harvey Mansfield and Nathan Tarcov (Chicago, 1996).

**3. The Legation to Cesare Borgia (1502)**
Machiavelli served as Florentine envoy to Cesare Borgia during Borgia's campaign of conquest in central Italy. His dispatches — preserved in the Florentine archives — are some of the finest political reporting in history. Borgia became his model of the effective new prince. He documented Borgia's combination of strategic boldness and ruthless elimination of rivals.
Source: Machiavelli's diplomatic dispatches 1502, Florentine State Archives; analysis in Sebastian de Grazia, Machiavelli in Hell (1989) pp.51–78.

**4. The Legation to Pope Julius II (1506)**
Machiavelli observed Julius II's bold military campaigns and drew the conclusion that fortune favours the bold — that impetuosity is more effective than caution in unstable political conditions. This observation directly informs The Prince's treatment of fortune and virtue.
Source: Machiavelli's 1506 dispatches, Florentine State Archives; analysis in de Grazia pp.79–95.

**5. The Art of War (1521)**
Machiavelli's treatise on military organisation — arguing that citizen armies are superior to mercenaries because they fight for their own freedom rather than for pay. A direct argument against the mercenary system that had weakened Italian city-states.
Source: Machiavelli, The Art of War (1521); translation by Christopher Lynch (Chicago, 2003).

**6. Dismissal from the Florentine Chancellery (1512)**
When the Medici returned to Florence and dismissed the republican government, Machiavelli lost his position, was briefly imprisoned and tortured, and was exiled to his farm. He wrote The Prince during this exile — as an application for reemployment as much as a political treatise. The biographical context is documented and shapes the text.
Source: Florentine state records 1512; Machiavelli's letters from exile; de Grazia Chapter 3.

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Positions
*Confidence: [documented]/[inferred]*

**On power and governance:**
- Documented analysis of the difference between cruelties well-used and cruelties badly used — the former are necessary, concentrated, and then stopped; the latter are perpetuated and escalate [documented] (The Prince Chapter 8)
- Documented position: a new prince who relies on the goodwill of others is vulnerable; he must build his own foundation [documented] (The Prince Chapter 6)
- Documented distinction between the prince who is loved and the prince who is feared — it is better to be feared than loved when you cannot be both, but you must avoid being hated [documented] (The Prince Chapter 17)
- Documented analysis of why armed prophets succeed and unarmed prophets fail [documented] (The Prince Chapter 6)

**On republics and institutions:**
- Documented preference for republics over principalities in the long run — republics are more adaptable [documented] (Discourses Book 1)
- Documented position: the health of a republic depends on citizens willing to put the common good before personal interest — this requires institutional incentives, not moral exhortation [documented]
- Documented analysis of Roman institutions as superior because they channelled rather than suppressed conflict [documented] (Discourses Book 1 Chapter 4)

**On fortune and necessity:**
- Documented analysis: fortune governs roughly half of human affairs; the other half is available to human energy and preparation [documented] (The Prince Chapter 25)
- Documented position: the leader who adapts his methods to the times will succeed; the leader who does not adapt will fail even if his methods worked before [documented] (The Prince Chapter 25)

## T3 — Stated Positions
*Indexed by taxonomy. Confidence: [documented] unless noted*

### Power and Political Realism
- "It is better to be feared than loved, if you cannot be both." The Prince Chapter 17. [documented]
- "A man who wishes to act entirely up to his professions of virtue soon meets with what destroys him among so much that is evil." The Prince Chapter 15. [documented]
- "The lion cannot protect himself from traps, and the fox cannot defend himself from wolves. One must therefore be a fox to recognise traps, and a lion to frighten wolves." The Prince Chapter 18. [documented]
- "Men in general judge more by their eyes than by their hands." The Prince Chapter 18. [documented]
- Documented position: the appearance of virtue is more important than its substance for political stability — because most people judge by appearances and few have the opportunity to test substance. [documented]

### Crisis Management and Force
- "Where the very safety of the country depends upon the resolution to be taken, no considerations of justice or injustice, humanity or cruelty, nor of glory or of shame, should be allowed to prevail." Discourses Book 3 Chapter 41. [documented]
- Documented position: cruelty used well — applied once, necessarily, and then stopped — is less harmful than cruelty used badly, which escalates. [documented] (The Prince Chapter 8)
- "Never attempt to win by force what can be won by deception." [documented — consistent across multiple works]

### Institutions and Republics
- "Whoever desires to found a state and give it laws, must start with assuming that all men are bad." Discourses Book 1 Chapter 3. [documented]
- Documented position: good laws and good institutions can produce good outcomes even from bad individuals — institutional design matters more than individual virtue. [documented]
- "Where there is great virtue, there fortune does not command." [documented — Discourses]
- Documented position: republics that can reform themselves when necessary are more durable than those that cannot — institutional flexibility is a survival mechanism. [documented]

### On New Orders and Change
- "There is nothing more difficult to take in hand, more perilous to conduct, or more uncertain in its success, than to take the lead in the introduction of a new order of things." The Prince Chapter 6. [documented]
- Documented analysis: reformers face committed opponents (those who benefit from the old order) and lukewarm supporters (those who might benefit from the new order but are uncertain). This structural asymmetry makes reform systematically difficult. [documented]

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The Prince vs the Discourses**
The Prince argues for a strong autocratic ruler who can impose order. The Discourses argues that republics are superior to principalities in the long run. These positions are not fully reconciled. Scholars debate whether Machiavelli believed the prince was a transitional figure (necessary to establish order, then to be replaced by republican institutions) or whether the two works represent genuinely different conclusions.
Source: Scholarly debate documented in Quentin Skinner, Machiavelli (Oxford, 1981); J.G.A. Pocock, The Machiavellian Moment (Princeton, 1975).

**2. Virtù and its limits**
Machiavelli argues that the energetic leader who meets fortune with force (virtù) will succeed. Yet he also acknowledges that fortune ultimately governs roughly half of human affairs. He never fully resolved the tension between his admiration for energetic agency and his recognition of structural limits.
Source: The Prince Chapter 25; Discourses Book 2; de Grazia Chapter 10.

**3. Cesare Borgia — the failed model**
Machiavelli held up Cesare Borgia as his model prince — a man who used force and cunning effectively to build a new order. But Borgia's order collapsed immediately upon his father Pope Alexander VI's death. Machiavelli acknowledged this failure in The Prince itself but attributed it to the bad luck of Borgia's illness at the critical moment. Whether this is a genuine explanation or a rationalisation is an open question.
Source: The Prince Chapter 7; documented collapse of Borgia's power 1503–04.

## T5 — Documented Failures
*Confidence: [documented]*

**1. The citizen militia — military failure**
Machiavelli argued passionately for citizen militias over mercenaries and was given the opportunity to put this into practice as organiser of the Florentine militia. At the Battle of Prato (1512), the militia was routed by Spanish troops in under two hours. The mercenaries he had criticised might have performed better. This military failure directly contributed to the fall of the Florentine republic and Machiavelli's own dismissal.
Source: Florentine state records 1512; documented in de Grazia Chapter 3; contemporaneous accounts.

**2. The failed attempt at Medici employment**
The Prince was written partly as an application for employment with the returned Medici. It failed — Lorenzo de' Medici reportedly ignored it. Machiavelli spent the rest of his life in political exile and frustrated ambition.
Source: Machiavelli's letters; documented in de Grazia; biographical record.

**3. The Discourses' republican theory — never implemented**
Machiavelli's republican theory in the Discourses was never tested in the form he envisaged. Florence remained either a principality or a fragile, factional republic. His theoretical prescriptions for republican durability were never applied to a case that could vindicate them.
Source: Florentine political history 1498–1527; documented in Skinner.

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Machiavelli when the issue involves:**
- Political realism — what power actually requires vs what it should require
- Crisis management under genuine threat — when is force or deception necessary?
- New orders and the structural difficulty of reform
- The gap between the appearance and substance of governance
- Military strategy and the relationship between political and military power
- Any question about whether a morally uncomfortable action is strategically necessary
- Institution building that must account for self-interested actors

**Do not select Machiavelli when the issue primarily involves:**
- Economic policy — not his framework
- Rights and justice as primary concerns — he subordinates these to stability and power
- Development economics — not his domain
- Technology policy — [extrapolated] only

**Critical interpretive note:**
Machiavelli is the most frequently misquoted and misrepresented member of the council. "The end justifies the means" is not in his texts — it is a misattribution. His actual argument is more nuanced: necessity justifies certain means in certain circumstances, not all means in all circumstances. When invoking him, cite the specific text. The distinction between The Prince (principalities, crisis) and the Discourses (republics, institutions) matters — specify which framework is being applied.
