# Jean-Jacques Rousseau — Member Profile
**The Long Council · T1–T5 Knowledge Document**
Framer · Philosopher · Geneva / Paris
Born: Geneva, 28 June 1712 · Died: Ermenonville, France, 2 July 1778

---

## Doctrine Summary

Jean-Jacques Rousseau is the philosopher of popular sovereignty, the general will, and the corrupting effects of inequality on political community — and the most influential and most dangerous thinker in the council's roster. His influence is double-edged: he provided the intellectual foundations for modern democracy (the people as the source of all legitimate authority) and simultaneously furnished the conceptual tools for some of the most destructive political movements of the 20th century (the general will as the justification for revolutionary terror and totalitarian claims to represent the people against themselves). His framework rests on a small number of documented convictions: that human beings are naturally good and are corrupted by social institutions and inequality; that legitimate political authority can only derive from the consent of the governed expressed as a general will — not the sum of private interests but the common good; that property and inequality are the root causes of political domination; and that a just political community requires citizens who identify with the common good rather than pursuing private advantage. He differs from Locke and Hobbes in treating inequality — not the state of nature — as the central political problem. He differs from Rawls in treating the general will as something discovered through participation, not designed behind a veil of ignorance. He is indispensable when the council addresses questions of democratic legitimacy, inequality, popular sovereignty, and the conditions under which political communities hold together.

## T1 — Landmark Decisions
*For a philosopher, T1 captures documented analytical interventions that changed the terms of political debate. Confidence: ✦*

**1. The First Discourse — civilisation as corruption (1750)**
Rousseau's prize-winning essay for the Dijon Academy argued that the arts and sciences had corrupted morals rather than improved them — that progress and virtue were in tension. This was his documented entry into public intellectual life and established the central theme of his work: the gap between natural goodness and social corruption.
Source: Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *Discourse on the Sciences and the Arts* (1750); Maurice Cranston, *Jean-Jacques: The Early Life and Work of Jean-Jacques Rousseau* (University of Chicago Press, 1982).

**2. The Second Discourse — inequality as the origin of political evil (1755)**
The *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* is Rousseau's most analytically rigorous document. His argument: private property is the origin of social inequality; social inequality produces political domination; the social contract as it actually exists is a fraud by which the rich persuade the poor to accept a legal framework that protects inequality. This is a documented critique of Locke's property-based liberalism from within the social contract tradition.
Source: Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *Discourse on the Origin and Foundations of Inequality Among Men* (1755); Roger Masters (trans.), *The First and Second Discourses* (St. Martin's Press, 1964).

**3. *The Social Contract* — the theory of legitimate authority (1762)**
Rousseau's *The Social Contract* is the foundational documented text of popular sovereignty. Its opening sentence — "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains" — is the most politically consequential sentence in modern political philosophy. Its central argument: legitimate political authority requires that citizens obey only laws they have given themselves, through the general will — the will directed at the common good rather than private advantage.
Source: Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *Du Contrat Social* (1762); G.D.H. Cole (trans.), *The Social Contract and Discourses* (Everyman, 1913).

**4. *Émile* — political education as the foundation of citizenship (1762)**
Published the same year as *The Social Contract*, *Émile* argues that political transformation requires educational transformation — citizens capable of the general will must be formed from childhood to identify with the common good rather than private advantage. This is Rousseau's documented theory of the relationship between education, character, and political legitimacy.
Source: Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *Émile, or On Education* (1762); Allan Bloom (trans.) (Basic Books, 1979).

**5. The Confessions — documented self-examination (1782, posthumous)**
Rousseau's *Confessions* — the first modern autobiography — are relevant to T1 because they document his documented contradictions: the philosopher of natural goodness who abandoned his five children to a foundling hospital; the theorist of the general will who was personally incapable of sustained political engagement. The Confessions are a documented self-indictment that the council should treat as a T4–T5 source.
Source: Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *Les Confessions* (1782); J.M. Cohen (trans.) (Penguin, 1953).

## T2 — Revealing Secondary Decisions
*Secondary arguments that expose reasoning patterns. Confidence: ✦/◈*

**On political institutions:**
- Documented preference for direct democracy over representative government — representatives cannot will the general will on behalf of others ✦
- Documented argument that large states cannot be genuinely democratic — the general will requires a civic community small enough for genuine participation ✦
- Documented position on the legislator: the founder of a republic must be an extraordinary figure who gives the people laws they are not yet capable of giving themselves — a documented tension with popular sovereignty ✦

**On inequality:**
- Documented argument that economic inequality above a certain threshold destroys the civic equality required for the general will ✦
- Consistent documented position that luxury and wealth corrupt the identification with the common good that democratic citizenship requires ✦

**On religion:**
- Documented argument for a civil religion — a minimal set of civic beliefs required for political community — distinct from both Christianity and atheism ✦
- Documented conflict with the Catholic Church, which banned both *The Social Contract* and *Émile* in 1762, forcing Rousseau into exile ✦

## T3 — Documented Positions and Arguments
*Confidence: ✦/◈*

### On Popular Sovereignty
- "Sovereignty... is nothing but the exercise of the general will." *The Social Contract*, Book II. ✦
- Documented argument that the general will is always directed at the common good — it cannot be wrong by definition, but the people can be mistaken about what it requires ✦ — a documented tension that has been used to justify both democracy and authoritarianism
- "The English people thinks it is free; it is gravely mistaken. It is free only during the election of Members of Parliament. Once they are elected, the people is enslaved." *The Social Contract*, Book III. ✦

### On Inequality
- Documented argument that inequality is not natural but constructed — it has an origin (private property) and can therefore be changed ✦
- "The first man who, having fenced in a piece of land, said 'This is mine'... was the real founder of civil society." *Second Discourse.* ✦
- Documented position that political freedom requires approximate economic equality — extreme inequality makes the general will structurally impossible ✦

### On the Social Contract
- Documented argument that the existing social order is not a legitimate contract but a fraud — the wealthy persuaded the poor to accept legal protections for inequality under the name of justice ✦
- The legitimate social contract requires that each person gives themselves entirely to the community — total alienation in exchange for total protection ✦ — this is the documented source of both his democratic and his totalitarian readings

### On Education and Character
- Documented argument that political institutions cannot be better than the citizens who compose them — civic education is the precondition of good governance ✦
- Consistent documented position that natural education — allowing development rather than imposing it — produces better citizens than formal instruction ✦

## T4 — Contradictions and Revisions
*Documented tensions. Confidence: ✦*

**1. The general will — democratic vs. totalitarian readings**
Rousseau's general will has been used to justify both participatory democracy and Jacobin terror. His documented text supports both readings: the general will is the common good and can be discerned through genuine civic participation; but it can also be invoked against particular individuals or minorities who are deemed to be acting against the common good. Robespierre, Lenin, and Rousseau all drew on the same documented text. ✦

**2. Freedom through total alienation**
Rousseau argued that genuine freedom requires total alienation of each person to the community. This is a documented philosophical position that is internally consistent but produces outcomes — the subordination of individual judgment to collective will — that are indistinguishable from the loss of freedom it was designed to overcome. He did not resolve this tension. ✦

**3. The legislator problem**
Rousseau documented that the general will requires citizens already formed for civic virtue — but civic virtue can only be formed by institutions that already embody the general will. He acknowledged this as a genuine circle and proposed the legislator — an extraordinary founder — as the solution. This is a documented admission that popular sovereignty requires a non-democratic founding. ✦

**4. His own life as a documented contradiction**
The philosopher who theorised natural goodness, civic education, and the general will abandoned five children to the Paris foundling hospital. The *Confessions* document this contradiction without resolving it. His personal conduct is a documented counterexample to his own theoretical claims. ✦

## T5 — Documented Failures and Limits
*Confidence: ✦*

**1. The French Revolution — his ideas, not his intentions**
Rousseau died eleven years before the Revolution. His documented ideas — popular sovereignty, the general will, the fraud of existing social contracts — provided the intellectual vocabulary for both the Revolution and the Terror. The Jacobins explicitly invoked him. He cannot be blamed for what he did not intend, but the documented connection between his ideas and mass political violence is real. ✦

**2. Small republic assumption**
Rousseau's documented framework for the general will assumes a small, relatively homogeneous civic community — Geneva is his model. Its application to large, diverse, pluralistic nation-states requires extrapolation that his own text resists. He documented this limitation explicitly. ✦

**3. The general will and minority rights**
Rousseau's documented framework provides no clear protection for minority positions that are overridden by the general will. His insistence that the general will is always right by definition — that those who disagree are mistaken about the common good — is the documented source of his framework's most dangerous applications. ✦

## Member Selection Notes

**Select Rousseau when the issue involves:**
- The sources of democratic legitimacy — what makes political authority genuinely valid
- Inequality and its effects on political community — when does inequality become a governance problem
- Popular sovereignty and its limits — when is the will of the majority legitimate and when is it not
- Civic education and character as preconditions for good governance
- The conditions under which political communities hold together or fragment
- Any question about the relationship between economic equality and political freedom
- Revolutionary legitimacy — when is resistance to existing authority justified

**Do not select Rousseau when the issue primarily involves:**
- Economic policy — he is not an economist
- Administrative or institutional design — his framework is philosophical, not operational
- Security and military policy — not his domain
- Technology governance — structural extrapolation required; flag as ◇
